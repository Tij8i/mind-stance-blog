*In this post I explore what self-reliance means to me, and why I see it as one of the most important traits a person can develop.*

Self-reliance has been a recurring theme in my life. In business, in language learning, in planning my own projects or managing relationships, I’ve always found that the moments when things moved forward were the moments when I was relying on myself not in isolation, but in clarity. To me, self-reliance means believing that you have what it takes to move forward, to improve, to create a better situation, without depending on anyone else to make it happen. It’s a mental stance, a belief that everything you need to succeed, to be fulfilled, to progress, is already available to you in some form. The gap between where you are and where you want to be can be filled by your own action, discipline, creativity, and persistence.

It’s not about perfection or pretending you have all the answers. It’s about refusing to stop acting because something seems to be missing. It’s about not waiting for the perfect condition, the perfect support, or the perfect timing. Self-reliance is what keeps you in motion. It’s what pushes you to try again, to find new ways, to adapt. It’s the opposite of victimhood.

That doesn’t mean I live in a constant state of self-reliance. Like anyone else, I falter. I get discouraged, I lose direction. But I try to use those moments as checkpoints. I ask myself: am I being self-reliant right now, or am I expecting someone or something else to do the work for me? That simple question helps me recalibrate. It’s not about guilt; it’s about awareness.

I also tend to look for this trait in people I spend time with, friends, colleagues, and partners. Not because I expect everyone to “do everything alone,” but because self-reliant people bring a different kind of energy into a room. Conversations with them are constructive. They focus on what can be done. They take responsibility. They don’t waste time blaming, waiting, or hiding behind circumstances.

There’s a paradox in self-reliance that I find beautiful. On one hand, it’s about putting yourself at the center of the action,  taking ownership. On the other hand, it’s about removing yourself from the picture altogether. You’re not the problem that needs fixing. You’re the instrument through which things get done. You don’t need to be different; you just need to act.

That emphasis on action is also why I see self-reliance as a cornerstone in business. Teams and organizations that cultivate it tend to thrive. They design roles that are interdependent but not dependent, what I like to call “mutually self-reliant and mutually supportive.” In these environments, people are trusted to think, decide, and execute within their own sphere, while staying aligned with the collective goal. It creates a culture of autonomy and accountability rather than dependency and blame.

From an organizational perspective, self-reliance is not easy to measure or hire for. Everyone likes to believe they’re self-reliant. Ask in an interview, and almost everyone will say yes. The real insight comes when someone can tell you *where* they’re not self-reliant, the moments when they lose confidence, need reassurance, or look outward instead of inward. That level of self-awareness is what actually reveals maturity. It shows they know their limits and are willing to work on them.

So my invitation, both to myself and anyone reading, is simple: pay attention to your relationship with self-reliance. Notice when you stand on your own and when you expect someone else to carry you. Notice what happens inside you when things don’t go your way — do you freeze, complain, or adapt? Cultivate this awareness. You don’t need to be perfectly self-reliant to benefit from it. You just need to remember that the ability to move forward is, and always has been, within you.
