There’s this idea I keep coming back to. I see it in Aikido, but I think it applies everywhere—in work, in parenting, in creativity, in growth.

It’s about the relationship between form and freedom.

In Aikido, when you first start, there’s structure everywhere. You don’t know where to put your feet, how to move, how to fall, how to breathe. It’s awkward. You follow. You imitate. You get things wrong. And slowly, you start to recognize patterns. Your body starts to remember things. The techniques become familiar. They’re no longer alien.

At that stage, everything is still very technical. You’re learning the “how.” You’re trying to be precise. And it’s important—it gives you a foundation. But it’s also rigid. You’re inside the form.

Eventually—if you stay long enough—something starts to shift.

You stop obsessing about every single movement. You stop freezing when something unexpected happens. You still use technique, but now you use it more fluidly. You start to move from feel. You start noticing space instead of fixating on correctness. You respond instead of remembering.

And then, one day, you move and you don’t really know where it came from. Your body just does it. It’s not a copy. It’s *yours*.

That moment is small, but it changes everything.

It’s like a signal: you’re not just learning the art anymore. You’re starting to embody it.

I think about that a lot—not just on the mat, but everywhere.

There’s a phase in almost everything I do where I’m inside the technique. Inside the structure. Trying to get it right. Trying to follow the steps. And then, if I keep going, there’s a moment where I stop thinking so much about “how” and start paying attention to “why.” Or maybe even just to “what now?”—in a real, present-tense sense.

And in that space, something more personal begins to emerge. A way of doing that belongs to me. That reflects my way of seeing and being, not just the system I learned from.

But that transition isn’t automatic. It’s easy to get stuck in the middle.

Stuck in the stage where you know the technique, but you haven’t integrated it. Where you’re still measuring yourself against some idea of how it’s “supposed” to look. Still trying to replicate someone else’s rhythm.

It takes time—and trust—to let go of the need to follow, and instead begin to improvise. To step into that space where what you do is still shaped by the form, but no longer confined by it.

In Aikido, you see this in how people move. Two students might perform the same technique, but you can tell who’s still in the mind and who’s already moved into the body. Into the moment. Into relationship. The technique is the same, but the quality is different. One is copying; the other is communicating.

It reminds me of other disciplines too. In cooking, for example, you can follow the recipe down to the gram. Or you can cook from memory, from taste, from intuition—because you’ve cooked it enough times to know where it’s going, what it needs, what can be adapted.

Same in music. Same in chess. Same in conversation. Same in leadership. Same in writing.

There’s a phase of precision, and then—if you let it happen—there’s a phase of ownership. Where what you’re doing becomes an expression, not just an execution.

And I think that’s where real growth lives.

Not just in adding more knowledge, but in integrating it into who you are.

Not just in mastering the rules, but in knowing when to set them aside.

This also explains a lot about why certain work or roles stop feeling alive after a while. It’s not that the work itself is wrong. It’s that you’ve outgrown the stage you’re in. You’ve learned the technique, but you’re not being given (or not giving yourself) the space to evolve past it. To make it your own.

We often think of mastery as the end of the road. But maybe mastery is just a new kind of beginning. A place where the form dissolves and the real learning starts again—because now you’re not just repeating, you’re *creating*.

In that sense, I’m trying to pay more attention to where I am in the learning arc of whatever I’m doing. Not to rush it, but to recognize it.

Am I still in the imitation phase?

Am I building muscle memory?

Am I starting to loosen the grip on technique?

Or am I holding on to structure longer than I need to, because it feels safer than taking ownership?

There’s no right pace. But there is a kind of honesty required.

Because once you’ve crossed into that deeper layer—once you *know* that you’re ready to move beyond the rigid form—staying inside it becomes constraining. It starts to feel performative. Disconnected. Slightly off.

And that’s the signal: it’s time to infuse more of yourself. To stop asking “how did they do it?” and start asking “how do *I* want to do it, now that I understand the essence?”

This is something I’ve been trying to apply not just in personal growth, but in business too.

There are best practices. Templates. Systems that work. But at some point, I have to stop trying to fit everything into a known model and start designing things that reflect how I work best. What kind of energy I want in the team. What kind of rhythm I want in the day. What kind of offer feels right—not just effective.

Because that’s the thing about structure—it helps you start. But it’s not meant to define the entire journey. If you stay in it too long, it can become another form of hiding. Another excuse not to trust yourself.

So this is where I’m at: somewhere between the form and the freedom. Still learning. Still integrating. Still letting go of the need to prove anything through precision. And instead, practicing showing up from a more honest place.

Because if growth is the goal, then ownership has to be part of it. Not just doing things well—but doing them in a way that feels *true*.

And maybe that’s what mastery really is.

Not perfection.

But alignment.
