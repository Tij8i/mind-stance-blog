*In this post I write about how the rational mind is a tool but not the orchestrator of life, why being present matters more than understanding, and how Aikido practice has shown me that doing is often wiser than thinking.*

##

I’ve come to realize that my mind is brilliant, but it’s not my master.

The brain is like any other organ, it has a specific function. Its job is to protect, to anticipate danger, to keep me alive. This was essential for a hunter-gatherer facing predators, cliffs, or poisonous plants. But in today’s world, survival often means something else: getting out of the comfort zone, entering controlled danger, sometimes just psychological danger. And that’s exactly where growth lives.

So while I trust my mind to calculate, analyze, and protect, I can’t let it run the whole show. If I do, I stay safe—but I also stay small. The mind draws boundaries. Life asks me to cross them.

### **When the brain becomes the boss**

Our culture worships thinking. Data, rationality, analysis, they’ve become the holy trinity of progress. And yes, they’ve brought us far. But when I look closer, I see the trap: planning is not doing.

How many people do I know who are brilliant at analyzing, debating, judging, even understanding, yet somehow never step forward? They can describe the problem with precision, but they live inside the problem. Talking replaces doing. Thinking replaces living.

This is the danger when the rational mind becomes the orchestrator. It asks for more information, more certainty, more control. But certainty is the enemy of movement.

### **The Aikido lesson**

Aikido has taught me this truth again and again.

When I first stepped on the mat, my instinct was to understand. To break down the technique, to name every step, to keep it rational. I wanted to “get” it—so I could control it. That’s the Western way: theory before practice.

But my teachers kept saying the opposite: forget about understanding. Just do it. Do it wrong, do it a thousand times, do it every way possible until your body starts to know what your mind cannot grasp.

At first, this was frustrating. How can I learn without understanding? But over time, repetition itself became the teacher. My body began to move with less thought, less tension. The technique became part of me, not a puzzle to be solved.

This is where Eastern practice differs. The lesson is not to figure out before doing, but to do until understanding appears. Knowledge is not given by explanation but by presence, by immersion.

### **Mushin: the state of no-mind**

In Japanese martial arts there is a term: *mushin*, the “no-mind” state. It doesn’t mean the absence of thought, but the absence of fixation. No overthinking, no self-consciousness—just being fully there.

When I first experienced even a glimpse of mushin, it was liberating. My body moved before I had time to comment on it. There was flow, clarity, simplicity. The mind was still present, but not in charge. It was like background music, not the conductor.

This is where practice in Aikido mirrors practice in life. Presence is what makes action possible. Rational thought can join later, to refine, to reflect, but not to paralyze.

### **The neuroscience whisper**

Modern science actually backs this up, though I don’t need science to believe what I’ve lived. Still, it’s reassuring to know.

Studies show that when we are fully present—in meditation, in flow, in mindful practice—parts of the brain linked to self-criticism and rumination quiet down. Other networks, the ones that help us notice the moment and adapt, light up.

In flow states, scientists talk about “transient hypofrontality”: the prefrontal cortex, the control center, takes a step back. Skill flows more easily when we’re not micromanaging ourselves. In other words: thinking less can make us more effective.

It’s the same wisdom my Aikido teachers whispered decades earlier: stop thinking, start moving.

### **Where do you live your life from?**

This is the real question: from where do I live my life?

If I live from thought alone, I end up second-guessing everything, analyzing endlessly, weighing every option until it’s too late to act.

If I live from presence, I move. I might still make mistakes, but mistakes become part of practice. Action brings feedback, feedback brings growth.

Life itself is not a thought experiment. It’s lived through the body, through choices, through movement.

### **Planning isn’t doing**

I love planning, don’t get me wrong. But I’ve learned to remind myself: planning isn’t doing.

There’s a simple shift I use now. When I catch myself analyzing too much, I ask: “What’s the smallest action I can take right now?” Not tomorrow, not after more thinking, but now.

Sometimes it’s writing the first line instead of outlining the whole book. Sometimes it’s stepping onto the mat instead of waiting until I feel “ready.” Sometimes it’s saying yes before I know every outcome.

Every time, presence shows me what the next step is. Rationality can catch up later.

### **Living as practice**

This, to me, is the ultimate stance: to see life as practice.

Just as in Aikido, I don’t need to “get it” before I start. I show up, I repeat, I fall, I adjust. My mind can observe, learn, and refine—but it’s not the playmaker.

Being present is not about throwing away thought. It’s about giving thought its proper place. Let the mind be an advisor, not the boss. Let the heart, the gut, the breath, the body—all of them—be part of the council.

Because growth doesn’t come from what I think about life. Growth comes from living it.

### **Conclusion**

So I return to the mat, again and again.

The mat reminds me that my rational mind is sharp, but presence is wiser. That analysis can point, but only action walks. That understanding is a fruit, not a seed.

And the more I practice, the more I see: this isn’t just about Aikido. It’s about everything.

Thinking is not doing. Presence is doing.

And life happens in the doing.
