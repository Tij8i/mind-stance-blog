*In this post, I explore the subtle but vital distinction between being “uncomfortable” and truly being in the process of growth. It’s not about romanticizing discomfort, but about tuning in to whether the discomfort we feel is pushing us forward—or just keeping us stuck.*

**“Discomfort is the price of admission to a meaningful life.”**

— Susan David

This line has stuck with me.

You hear variations of it everywhere—in Stoic philosophy, in entrepreneurial circles, in athletic coaching. But lately, I’ve been sitting with it in a different way. Not as a quote to share, but as a question to ask myself.

Am I truly growing? Or am I just tolerating discomfort?

That’s what this post is about.

## **A Dangerous Illusion: Getting Good at Discomfort Without Moving Forward**

I’ve always thought discomfort was a good sign.

When something felt a little hard, a little uncertain, a little outside my wheelhouse—I took that as a marker of progress. And to an extent, that’s true. Getting uncomfortable is necessary.

But here’s where things get tricky: it’s possible to be in discomfort and still not grow.

You can live in a constant low-grade discomfort. Stress. Frustration. Impatience. You might even be applauded for it—“He’s working so hard,” “She’s grinding every day.” But are you changing? Are you evolving?

We’ve become so used to glorifying pain, hustle, and anxiety that we sometimes forget discomfort is a *means*, not an *end*. It’s only valuable if it’s connected to movement. Otherwise, it’s just another flavor of stuck.

## **Where Are You Headed? The Need for a Reference Point**

In physics, motion is always defined in relation to something else. You need a point of reference to say you’re moving.

Same in life. Same in personal growth.

Discomfort without direction is just noise.

Discomfort with direction is a signal.

But to know if there’s direction, you need to have defined a before and after. A personal benchmark. Otherwise, you’re just spinning.

In coaching, we talk about goal setting a lot. But I prefer to think in terms of *vectors*. A vector has both direction and magnitude. It says: “Here’s where I was. Here’s where I’m headed. And here’s how far I’ve come.”

Ask yourself:

- What discomfort am I currently experiencing?

- What is it supposed to be moving me toward?

- How do I know I’m actually getting there?

This may sound abstract, but it’s actually very practical. If your goal is to become more confident, what is a situation where you’d notice a shift? If your goal is to get fitter, what are the early markers of progress? These signals matter more than the pain itself.

## **Why We Confuse Pain With Progress**

There are a few reasons why we fall into the trap of equating discomfort with growth:

- Social Narrative: We live in a culture that glamorizes struggle. The startup founder pulling all-nighters. The athlete pushing past the breaking point. The artist starving for their work. So we assume discomfort = depth. But what if it’s just trauma looping?

- Personal Bias: Many of us grew up learning that effort is inherently virtuous. So we value ourselves for how much we’re “trying.” But trying is not the same as transforming.

- Avoidance of Real Change: Ironically, staying in a “known” discomfort can be easier than taking the next leap. We get used to anxiety. We master coping. We stop moving.

Growth is not linear, but it *is* directional. You feel it when you’re stretching toward something that matters. Not when you’re sitting in an old discomfort with a new excuse.

## **The Litmus Test: Feedback Loops**

In systems theory, feedback loops are what keep a system healthy. Input, response, adjustment.

We need the same in our lives.

Are you getting real feedback from your discomfort?

In other words:

- Is your discomfort producing new insights?

- Is it changing your behavior?

- Is it affecting your results?

If not, then it’s not a growth discomfort—it’s a *stuck* discomfort.

Let me give you a personal example.

When I started public speaking, I was nervous—classic discomfort. But over time, I learned to read the room better. My pacing improved. My message landed more clearly. That was a feedback loop. I could tell I was growing.

Contrast that with a time when I was grinding through a job I didn’t love, telling myself it was “building resilience.” It wasn’t. It was draining me. No feedback, no change. Just white-knuckling through something I wasn’t meant to do in the first place.

## **The Role of Reflection: Discomfort vs. Alignment**

One tool that’s helped me immensely is regular self-check-ins. Every week, I ask myself:

- What felt hard this week?

- What did that hardness *teach me*?

- Did I move through it, or just carry it?

There’s an important distinction between growing pains and misalignment.

Misalignment feels like effort without expansion.

You push, but you don’t grow. You sacrifice, but don’t evolve. You stretch, but snap back.

Growth-aligned discomfort, on the other hand, expands your inner capacity. It humbles you. It teaches you. It gives back more than it takes.

Sometimes we need to zoom out. Map the week. Or even the month. Look for signs of movement. Are you becoming more of the person you want to be, or just surviving another cycle?

## **The Eastern Lens: Emptiness and Resistance**

Buddhist thought, especially Zen, often invites us to observe discomfort with detachment.

In meditation, when your legs hurt or your mind races, you don’t *resist*. You *observe*. You *accept*. But the goal isn’t to stay in pain forever. The goal is to loosen your identification with pain, and in doing so, learn to move beyond it.

I find this useful when navigating the murkier waters of emotional discomfort. For example, fear of rejection. Or creative blocks. These don’t always respond to effort. Sometimes, they yield to space.

As Bruce Lee said, “Don’t push the river. It flows by itself.”

In Western productivity culture, that’s radical. But in growth work, it’s wisdom. Sometimes, the growth is in *letting go*, not pushing harder.

## **Metaphor Time: The Gym, the Mat, the Mountain**

**The gym:** You lift weights, they tear your muscle fibers, you rest, and you grow stronger. Pain is part of the process—but only if recovery is included. Without recovery, it’s just injury.

**The mat:** In martial arts (I practice Aikido), discomfort is ever-present. But you’re not meant to fight it. You’re meant to *blend* with it. The goal isn’t dominance—it’s presence. Discomfort is information. When you learn to listen, you move better.

**The mountain:** Imagine hiking. You’re sweating, breathless, unsure. But you’re rising. The view keeps changing. The air gets thinner. There’s effort—but also elevation.

That’s growth discomfort. It doesn’t just wear you out. It *lifts you*.

## **What Growth Actually Feels Like**

Growth doesn’t always feel heroic.

Sometimes it’s awkward.

Sometimes it’s slow.

Sometimes it’s full of self-doubt.

But the key is that you’re not standing still.

Even if you can’t measure it in achievements yet, there are signs:

- You’re asking better questions.

- You’re setting firmer boundaries.

- You’re seeing old patterns with new eyes.

- You’re choosing what matters, more often than before.

It might not feel comfortable. But it feels *real*.

And realness is a sign of growth.

- Steven Pressfield – *The War of Art*

Not all discomfort is good. But the right kind, in the right dose, aligned with the right purpose—that’s where the gold lies.

## **Final Thought: Don’t Chase Pain. Chase Truth.**

You don’t need to make life harder than it is.

But you do need to get honest with yourself.

- Is your discomfort serving your growth?

- Or is it just the inertia of a life you’ve outgrown?

This distinction can change everything.

Growth isn’t about being tough. It’s about being true.

To yourself. To your direction. To the life you actually want to build.

And that… that’s worth getting uncomfortable for.
