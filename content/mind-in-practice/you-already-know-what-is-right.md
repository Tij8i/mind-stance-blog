*You’re already the expert of your own life. No one knows your path better than you. Logic helps, data convinces others, but intuition whispers truths only you can hear. Trust yourself. Tune in. You already know what’s right.*

## **My Thought in One Sentence**

We already are the experts of our own lives, which means the real task is not to look for external authority, but to listen more deeply to ourselves and trust the intuition that quietly guides us.

## **The Longer Reasoning**

We spend much of our lives looking outward. Searching for advice, strategies, and systems. We want to know how to live, how to succeed, how to be happy. We read books, watch videos, listen to podcasts, and follow gurus.

And yet, all along, there’s a truth so close we often miss it: we already know what’s right for us.

This is not a statement of arrogance. It’s not about rejecting learning, rejecting guidance, or rejecting the wisdom of others. It’s about understanding a fundamental shift: external input can inform us and inspire us, but it cannot replace the inner authority that only we can possess.

### **Rational Thinking vs. Intuition**

Rational thinking is valuable. It structures the world. It uses logic, models, and external validation. It allows us to communicate, compare, and make sense of reality in ways others can understand. In a team or society, logic is indispensable because it creates a common language.

But when it comes to your life, your choices, your growth, your meaning, logic is not enough. Logic operates with second-hand material: models of the external world. Intuition operates with first-hand material: *you*.

Intuition is not a mystical fog. It’s your nervous system, your subconscious, your embodied knowledge. As psychologist Gerd Gigerenzer puts it, intuition is “recognition-based” — it’s the pattern-matching ability of a mind that has seen, felt, and lived. Neuroscientists at the Greater Good Science Center describe it as “a form of rapid perception we’re wired for,”  fast, elegant, often more accurate than the slow grind of rational analysis.

Still, in today’s world, we undervalue it. Data is king. In product teams, you hear: *“In God we trust. Everyone else, bring data.”* That makes sense in governance, where alignment matters. But your mind is not a democracy. Inside you, one voice wins. The only question is: are you listening to the right one?

### **Why Intuition Is Harder to Trust**

The difficulty is that intuition often arrives without an argument. It comes as a hunch, a bodily sensation, a quiet urge. You can’t always explain it to others. Sometimes you can’t even explain it to yourself. That makes it uncomfortable in a culture that prizes explanation and justification.

But let’s flip the perspective. Rational thinking makes sense *after* the fact. Intuition makes sense *before* the fact. Logic analyzes the past and projects into the future. Intuition operates in the now.

If you’re already the expert of your own life, then trusting intuition is not optional — it’s essential.

## **The Role of Others**

This doesn’t mean ignoring others. Others are mirrors, sparks, sources of inspiration. But the truth is: you never receive them as they are. Communication is always corrupted by what you want to hear, what you’re ready to hear, what your own filters allow through.

That’s why the words of a teacher, friend, or book might strike you as useless one day — and years later, open an entirely new world. The content didn’t change. *You* did.

So the role of others is not to give you a final answer. Their role is to plant unfinished thoughts, to leave trails you might follow when you’re ready.

And that’s the beauty: only unfinished business is alive business. If you fully grasp something, you shelve it. It becomes trivia. If you don’t quite grasp it, it stays active. It gnaws at you. It makes you grow.

## **Accepting Responsibility**

To accept that you are the expert of your own life is to accept responsibility. It means no guru, no theory, no data set can carry the weight for you. You can learn, yes. You can be inspired. But ultimately, you must interpret.

And interpretation is never passive. The information you “receive” is always external input + internal processing. The same words will mean different things to different people, and to you at different times.

So the core responsibility is not to consume more, but to become more conscious of how you interpret. That’s the real power: to manage your own interpretations to your benefit.

## **Cultivating Intuition: A Practice**

Here’s the key: intuition is natural, but not automatic. It must be cultivated.

Research from Berkeley’s Greater Good Science Center shows that people who regularly connect with themselves — through quiet reflection, journaling, meditation, or simply unplugging — develop sharper, more reliable intuition. Psychologists call it “creating the conditions for inner listening.”

Think of it as tuning a radio. The signal is always there. But if you never slow down, you only hear static.

Here are a few simple ways to practice:

- Create quiet space daily. Even five minutes of silence can sharpen your inner ear.

- Notice bodily cues. Intuition often speaks through the body — tension in the gut, a sudden ease in the chest, a spark of energy.

- Reflect on past decisions. When did your gut prove right? When did you override it and regret it? Build trust by remembering your own evidence.

- Limit the noise. Too much input drowns out your signal. Sometimes the most intuitive thing you can do is log off, walk outside, and breathe.

As Laura Huang, Harvard professor and author, says: *intuition is not the opposite of data — it’s what helps us navigate when data isn’t enough.*

## **Concrete Implications**

Keeping in mind that we are already the experts of our lives leads to some practical conclusions:

- There is nothing to “learn” in the traditional sense. Growth is not about stuffing more concepts into your head, but eliminating noise and reconnecting to yourself.

- Intuition trumps data when it comes to the inner world. Logic is useful for explaining to others, but intuition is your compass for what works for you.

- Others inspire, but they cannot decide for you. What doesn’t speak to you today may tomorrow. Stay open, but don’t force it.

- You are responsible for meaning-making. Receiving information is never neutral — you filter, interpret, and create meaning. Own this process.

## **Closing**

“You already know what’s right.”

That’s the message. Not that you should stop learning. Not that you should stop seeking wisdom. But that you should reframe where the authority lies.

Knowledge from outside is like light from a lamp. Useful, illuminating. But it only matters if your eyes are open, if you’re looking, if you’re ready. The lamp cannot see for you.

Trust that inner readiness. Cultivate it. Listen for it. Because in the end, intuition is not a mystery, not a gamble — it’s your own life speaking to you.

And no one knows your life better than you.
