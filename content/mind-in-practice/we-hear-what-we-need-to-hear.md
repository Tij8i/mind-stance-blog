*We don’t always hear what is said. We hear what we are ready for. The same words fall differently depending on where we are in our journey.*

### **The intuition**

We hear what we need to hear. The right words, the right meaning, at the right time.

### **Selective attention at play**

Our attention is selective. This is not just a figure of speech—it’s how the human brain works. Psychology calls it *selective attention*: the ability to filter out the endless flood of sensory input and focus on a fraction of it. Without this filter we would drown in noise.

But this filter is not neutral. It is shaped by our beliefs, expectations, emotional state, and even our unconscious biases. NLP (Neuro-Linguistic Programming) talks about this in terms of *filters*. We delete, distort, and generalize information as it comes in. We do not take in reality “as it is.” We take in the version of reality that our mind is prepared to handle.

Two people can listen to the same argument and come out with entirely different impressions. One hears encouragement. Another hears criticism. One picks up the detail, another the big picture. The words themselves do not carry the difference. It’s the filters that do.

And this is not a flaw. It is the way our mind protects us, helping us process the world one layer at a time.

### **My Aikido example**

In Aikido, I’ve lived this truth again and again. From my very first class, my Sensei emphasized:

> “Aikido is not about the forms. It is about blending with your partner, staying centered, perceiving what is going on around you.”

Those words landed in my ears, but not in my body. At the beginning, my entire attention was consumed by how to place my feet, where to put my hands. Staying centered was an abstraction.

As weeks went by, once the basic movements started to become less clumsy, I began to notice timing and distance. Then I realized that even timing and distance collapse if my body is not stable. Step by step, the same feedback opened new doors.

Months later, my Sensei repeated the same line: focus on your centered stance before anything else. This time, it struck differently. I now had *ears to hear it*. The words were unchanged. My perception was not.

This is the cycle. We hear what we need to hear at the stage we are in. And then we hear it again later, and it means something else. The same sentence, the same advice, unfolds in layers over time.

### **How the mind filters words**

When we hear something, the words first hit our senses. But between sound and meaning lies an invisible filter: our mental model of the world.

- Deletion: we leave out pieces of information that don’t fit or that we’re not ready to handle.

- Distortion: we twist the meaning to align with our existing beliefs.

- Generalization: we reduce a complex message to a simple rule that feels manageable.

NLP uses this model to explain why communication is never about “what is said” but always about “what is received.”

Think of it as a radio. The station is broadcasting a wide signal, but your tuner is set to one frequency. You don’t hear the rest of the spectrum, only what you are tuned for.

This is not bad news. It means our minds are always cooperating with our growth. They give us just enough to process in the moment.

### **Psychology and readiness**

Psychology has its own language for this. Carl Rogers, the humanistic psychologist, spoke of *congruence*: we can only integrate new insights if they match, at least partly, with our current self-concept. If they are too far away, we either reject them or distort them until they fit.

Similarly, developmental theorists like Robert Kegan suggest that humans grow in *orders of consciousness*. Each order allows us to make sense of reality in a certain way. Advice that fits one order will be nonsense at another. When we shift to a new order, the same advice suddenly glows with new meaning.

This is why the “right decision, right time” matters. What sounds like empty words today may become a guiding principle tomorrow.

### **Listening differently**

The practical takeaway is simple: stop demanding complete clarity from every sentence you hear.

Often, we rush to judge. Is this right? Is this wrong? Does it fit my logic? But growth doesn’t always work on logic’s timeline. Words don’t need to be perfectly understood to be useful. They need to land in a way that makes sense for *you, now*.

Try listening with a different stance:

- Instead of asking *“is this true?”*, ask *“what does this mean for me in this moment?”*

- Instead of asking *“is this the full picture?”*, ask *“What small piece can I use right now?”*

Words are not puzzles to be solved. They are raw materials. What matters is how they interact with your filters, your readiness, your state.

### **Everyday examples**

Think of everyday life:

- A child hears “be kind.” At first, it means share toys. Years later, it means something about compassion in relationships. Later still, it expands to kindness toward oneself. Same phrase, new depth each time.

- In business, a mentor says “focus on the fundamentals.” To a beginner, it means do the basics well. To someone experienced, it may mean strip away complexity and return to the essence.

- In relationships, the advice “listen more” first sounds like keeping quiet. Later, it means hearing the emotion behind the words.

Each time, the words are identical. The meaning evolves because *we evolve*.

### **Back to Aikido**

When I bow at the start of class now, I know my Sensei will repeat things I have already heard a hundred times. But I don’t roll my eyes anymore. I ask myself: what layer will I hear today?

It is humbling. It teaches me patience. It reminds me that understanding is not a one-time event, but a spiral. Each loop brings me back to the same words with a new perspective.

### **Closing**

We hear what we need to hear. Not more, not less.

This is not a limitation. It is a rhythm. A partnership between our mind and our growth.

The right words, the right decision, the right moment—these align when we are ready. And if we are not ready, the words will wait. They will circle back, again and again, until one day we finally have ears to hear them.

So next time you catch yourself thinking *“I already know this,”* pause. Ask instead: *“What does this mean for me now?”*

Because the lesson is not in the words. The lesson is in the timing.
