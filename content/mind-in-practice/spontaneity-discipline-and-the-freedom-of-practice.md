*In this post, I explore the apparent tension between spontaneity and discipline, and why I believe that centering your life around “practice” resolves that tension. I’m not writing this because I’ve mastered it, I’m writing it because I’m walking it. These are my struggles, my thoughts, and the system I’ve started to build to find freedom within commitment.*

The thought

We think discipline kills spontaneity. Or that spontaneity sabotages consistency. But what if both become natural when you’re aligned with your practice?

The longer reasoning

Let me start with a disclaimer: I’m writing about this not because I’ve figured it out. I’m writing because I’m learning to see it more clearly, and maybe sharing it helps you see something in your own path, too.

Here’s what I’ve noticed in myself. I tend to fill my days with routines. When something works, my instinct is: this is great, I need to do it regularly. So I open my calendar, schedule a block, and—more often than not—that’s where it ends. I don’t follow through. It’s not that I don’t believe in it. It’s just that life, as I live it, doesn’t play by those rules.

Example: I built a simple daily check-in—a 5-minute form to ask myself how I slept, what I’m feeling, what’s on my mind. I loved the idea. I set it up. I added it to my morning routine. I haven’t touched it in over two weeks.

Why? Because some mornings my kids wake up early and need me. Other mornings I wake up with a fire to write and I want to follow that. Or I’m packing for a trip. Or I’m already on a call. My days don’t form a neat grid. And I’m starting to see that maybe they’re not meant to.

There was a time I wondered if this was just a discipline issue—if I should set firmer boundaries, wake earlier, enforce the system. But in truth? I like my life the way it is. It moves. It changes. It asks me to stay responsive. And I trust that when something is truly important, I *do* follow through.

What I’ve started doing instead is this:

I separate tools from practice.

I have a list of activities that help me ground myself, stay aware, perform better—like that check-in form. But I don’t treat them as obligations. I treat them like a menu. I check in with myself in the morning, see what’s available, and I choose what fits.

It’s like going to the gym. One day you feel like lifting, another you just want to stretch. Both serve the practice of showing up and staying well. The deeper discipline is not in following a schedule—it’s in staying aligned to the reason you went in the first place.

This is the shift

Most systems out there suggest something like this:

- Set your goals

- Build routines to serve them

- Use discipline to stick to the routines

- Stay flexible enough to adapt

That’s useful, to an extent. But I’m advocating something different:

- Start with your practice. Your “why.” What you care about. What kind of human you’re trying to be.

- From there, select your tools. But don’t bind them into rigid routines unless they want to become rituals.

- Use discipline to stay in relationship with the practice. Not with a calendar entry.

It’s a subtle but radical shift.

Instead of trying to fit your life into routines, let your practice shape your choices.

References and angles

Many thinkers have danced with this tension before.

- The Stoics, like *Musonius Rufus*, saw discipline as the key to living in alignment with reason and virtue. For them, self-restraint was not about routines—it was about staying true to principle.

- *Cecil Touchon*, a visual artist, wrote about “rituals of showing up” that leave room for improvisation. He starts his creative work at a set time—but what happens after is open, fluid.

- In cognitive psychology, there’s a helpful split between convergent and divergent thinking. Convergent thinking (discipline) helps us narrow, refine, decide. Divergent thinking (spontaneity) helps us explore, invent, disrupt. You need both. But the trick is *when* you engage each—and *why*.

- Neuroscience backs this up too: structured habits free up cognitive energy so creativity can flourish. But too much structure limits serendipity—what some researchers call the “default mode network,” the mind’s playground for spontaneous insight.

You can also look at martial arts—especially Aikido—where form and flow coexist. The kata (form) is fixed. But the uke’s attack? That’s always new. You train the form not to control the moment, but so you’re free to meet the moment skillfully.

The metaphor

Think of a jazz musician. They’ve practiced their scales a thousand times. They know the chord progressions by heart. But when the solo comes, they don’t think—they play. Improvisation is not the opposite of discipline. It’s what becomes possible *because* of it.

But here’s the twist in my view:

The *real* practice isn’t the scales. It’s not the daily warmup. It’s the musician’s *relationship to the music*. That’s what I’m talking about here. That’s the anchor. The rest—the tools, the rhythms, the rituals—they orbit around that.

Conclusion: The discipline of staying in practice

I’m still working this out. I still get caught in routines that feel like cages. I still make plans I never follow through on. But when I come back to the question—what is my practice? I find clarity again.

The key is not to lock your life into a system.

The key is to live in a relationship with your practice.

Let your tools support you, not bind you.

Let your calendar reflect your intentions, not dictate them.

Let discipline serve your practice.

Let spontaneity serve your presence.

And most importantly:

Let your practice serve *you* your growth, your freedom, your way of walking through this life.
