* In this post, I reflect on the old saying “a person convinced against their will remains of the same opinion still.” I share how it shaped my approach to sales, why listening and acceptance matter more than pressure, and why integrity in influence leads to growth, both personal and professional.*

### **Intuition**

The first time I heard this quote, it stopped me. It was one of those phrases that lands in your mind and never really leaves. For me, it wasn’t just about persuasion; it was about life. And it’s one of the reasons I stepped into the world of personal development in the first place.

### **The Seed of Reflection**

Even as a kid, before I knew what “personal development” meant, I was drawn to philosophy and psychology. I remember an older classmate trying to explain philosophy to me before I had my first class. He told me, with a smirk, that philosophy is useless because it teaches you that everything can be questioned and turned upside down. To him, it was confusion. To me, it was fascinating. The idea that reality could bend depending on perspective—that relativity and mental agility—was exciting.

That openness is something I still value today. Unfortunately, the world of business often runs on very different fuel. Business wants clarity, action, outcomes. It thrives on short-term pragmatism. Socratic questioning, open-ended inquiry, and uncertainty rarely find a comfortable home there.

This contrast—the agility of philosophy vs. the urgency of business—has shaped how I see the quote.

### **Force vs. Alignment**

*A person convinced against their will remains of the same opinion still.*

The meaning is simple: when you push someone into something they don’t truly want, nothing changes inside them. The external “yes” is a disguise for the internal “no.”

In business, this shows up everywhere. Yes, you can pressure someone into buying. You can play on urgency, trick them with scarcity, or hype up emotion for an impulse decision. You might even win the short-term sale. But give it time. Their doubts resurface. They regret. And when they look back, they may see you as clever, professional, maybe even “a top salesperson.” But not as someone who cared for them. Not as someone aligned with their interests.

That gap—between short-term force and long-term alignment—is where morality enters the game.

### **The Morality of Sales**

Sales has a reputation problem. Many people see it as manipulation, as a game of psychological chess where the buyer’s best interest is optional. That’s not the stance I want to take.

To me, sales are only sustainable when they’re moral. And morality begins with having the other person’s interest at heart. That’s not sentimental—it’s strategic. If you align your offer with what truly benefits the other person, you create trust, longevity, and partnership.

You don’t need to convince anyone “against their will.” You need to connect with their will.

### **Listening as Presence**

That connection starts with listening. Not the kind of listening where you’re waiting for a pause so you can deliver your next argument. Real listening.

Listening to what they say. Listening to what they don’t say. Listening to where they hesitate, where their energy rises or drops.

It’s easy to think: “They don’t really know what they need—I do.” Maybe that’s true. Maybe you have a perspective they lack. But your role isn’t to correct them. Your role is to serve them forward. Sometimes that means questioning. Sometimes it means confirming. Sometimes it means offering another lens. There isn’t one script. The stance that matters is service.

### **Acceptance as Detachment**

And then comes acceptance.

Sometimes the person simply isn’t ready. You may have something incredibly valuable for them. You may believe it could change their life or their business. But if they’re not ready to step in, you need to accept that.

This is not passive resignation. It doesn’t mean “do nothing.” It means acting without clinging. When someone you believe is a perfect fit still says “no,” you face a choice. Push harder—or stand grounded.

Standing grounded means you trust your message, your offer, and your presence. You let the “no” land. And then you reflect: how could I serve the next person better? How could I tell the story differently, show the picture more fully?

That’s growth.

### **Sales as Personal Development**

Every sales conversation is a mirror. It’s not just about them. It’s about you.

A rejection can feel like a wound. But it can also be feedback, sharpening your clarity. A difficult conversation can expose your impatience or your need for control. Each prospect is an opportunity for self-awareness.

This is why I see sales not as a battlefield to conquer but as a dojo to practice. The opponent isn’t the customer. The opponent is your own ego. The practice is staying present, detached, and focused on service.

### **Against “Always Be Closing”**

Traditional sales culture loves the mantra “Always Be Closing.” Close hard, close often, close fast.

My experience tells me the opposite. If you live by “always be closing,” you will always be forcing. And when you force, you may win the game but lose the person.

I prefer a different mantra: *Always Be Coaching.* Or even more simply: *Always Be Present.*

Presence doesn’t mean weakness. It means strength without force. It means confidence in your value, delivered without the need to manipulate. It means letting your integrity lead.

### **The Deeper Psychology**

Modern psychology backs this up. Leon Festinger’s theory of cognitive dissonance showed that when people act against their beliefs under pressure, they don’t necessarily change their beliefs—they feel discomfort and rationalize. But unless the change is chosen freely, the old opinion stays.

The quote, first written by Samuel Butler in the 1600s and later popularized by Dale Carnegie, anticipated this. True change is self-chosen. It can’t be imposed.

### **Metaphors to Remember**

- **Golf swing**: Force the club, and the ball hooks or slices. Let it flow, aligned with balance, and it lands true.

- **Aikido**: You don’t overpower an opponent—you redirect energy, staying centered. Influence works the same way.

- **Breath**: Try to force a breath, and it feels unnatural. Breathe with awareness, and the whole body relaxes into alignment.

Each metaphor repeats the same truth from a different angle: force backfires. Alignment lasts.

### **References & Follow-Ups**

- Samuel Butler, *Hudibras* (1663): “He that complies against his will, is of his own opinion still.”

- Dale Carnegie, *How to Win Friends & Influence People* (1936), which popularized the quote in modern business circles.

- Leon Festinger, *A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance* (1957).

- For practical application: Stephen Covey’s *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*—particularly Habit 5: *Seek first to understand, then to be understood.*

### **Conclusion**

This quote is more than an old saying. It’s a lens. It reminds me that true influence isn’t about pressing harder—it’s about standing present.

You don’t have to close every sale. You have to master the stance you bring into every conversation. Force belongs to the old economy of pressure and short-term gains. Presence belongs to the new economy of alignment, trust, and growth.

*A person convinced against their will remains of the same opinion still.* The wisdom hasn’t aged. It’s still the call to integrity. And it’s still the reminder: service, not pressure. Presence, not force. Growth, not conquest.
