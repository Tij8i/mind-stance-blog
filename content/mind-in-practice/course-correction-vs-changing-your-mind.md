*In this post I reflect on the subtle but important difference between staying the course, course correcting, and quietly changing your mind — and why time horizon is the missing variable that explains so much frustration.*

This morning I found myself circling around a distinction that I think matters more than we usually admit: the difference between course correction and changing your mind.

On paper, we all know the rules. You need a vision. A North Star. Something that pulls you forward when things get messy or uncomfortable. Persistence matters. Staying the course matters. We tend to admire people who don’t quit too easily, who don’t get distracted by every new opportunity or shiny idea.

At the same time, we’re also told that rigidity is dangerous. That we need to be flexible. That when circumstances change, we should be willing to adapt, adjust, and course correct rather than blindly sticking to a plan that no longer fits reality.

So far, all of this sounds reasonable. Almost obvious.

The problem starts when another behavior sneaks in under the same language: changing your mind.

Most people have experienced this, even if they don’t always name it that way. You start something with genuine intent. You invest energy. Then, days or weeks or months later, your attention shifts. Something else looks more interesting, more promising, more aligned. You pivot. You move on.

Some people don’t struggle much with this. Either they don’t feel the pull as strongly, or they’ve built systems that help them stay anchored. Others — myself included, for a long time — live right in the middle of this tension.

What I’ve come to see is that course correction and changing your mind are not the same thing, even though we often describe them using the same words.

Course correction means you’re still loyal to a direction. You’re adjusting how you move, not where you’re going. Changing your mind means the destination itself has shifted, even if you don’t consciously acknowledge it.

Where this gets tricky is in how we define our North Star in the first place.

If your goal is concrete and time-bound, the distinction is fairly clear. If I decide that I want a specific role, a specific promotion, within a specific timeframe, then it’s obvious what counts as course correction. Adjusting my approach, learning missing skills, changing how I allocate my time — those are tactical moves. Changing the goal entirely is a change of mind.

But many of us don’t operate with goals like that. We define our North Stars at a much higher level. Sometimes deliberately, sometimes because it feels more honest or more spacious.

“I want to be successful.”

“I want to be a successful entrepreneur.”

“I want a lifestyle business in something I care about.”

These are not bad goals. They’re not wrong. But they’re abstract enough that almost any move can be framed as progress toward them.

Quit one project to start another? Course correction — the first one wasn’t working.

Take a job to learn skills you don’t have yet? Course correction.

Step away entirely for a while to recover energy or perspective? Still a course correction.

At that level of abstraction, almost nothing ever counts as changing your mind. And to be clear, this isn’t necessarily self-deception. If your time horizon is your entire lifetime, then many of these moves are legitimate. Over a long enough arc, detours and reinventions are part of the path.

This is where time horizon becomes the missing variable.

Time horizon quietly determines whether something is a sensible course correction or an endless postponement of results. If the impact you’re aiming for is “sometime in my life,” then the range of acceptable moves is enormous. You can afford long learning phases. You can afford to reset multiple times. You can afford to wander.

But if you want results within a year — or even within a few months — then your vision has to compress. Your definition of course correction has to tighten. You don’t get infinite optionality anymore.

Looking back, I can see this pattern clearly in my own life. My North Stars were always high-level, values-driven, identity-based. I never experienced my shifts as changing my mind. I genuinely believed I was course correcting. And in a lifetime sense, maybe I was.

But those course corrections led me down long, winding roads that produced very little in the short term. They kept me busy, stimulated, learning — but not necessarily building momentum.

At some point, something changed. Waiting stopped feeling noble. Deferring results stopped feeling wise. I didn’t want to live entirely in the long arc anymore. I wanted traction. Feedback. Evidence that something was actually working now, not just theoretically aligned with who I wanted to become.

That forced a different kind of question. Not “does this fit my ultimate vision?” but “will this produce results within the time horizon I actually care about?”

That’s the shift.

It’s not about abandoning the North Star. It’s about grounding it enough that it starts to constrain you instead of just justifying everything. A North Star that’s too abstract gives you infinite permission and zero pressure. A North Star that’s time-bound forces trade-offs. It makes some options clearly wrong, even if they’re interesting or aligned in a vague sense.

When you move your focus closer to the present, course correction becomes sharper. It stops being a story you tell yourself and starts being a measurable adjustment. And changing your mind becomes easier to spot — not as a moral failure, but as a real choice with real consequences.

If you want results today, or this year, your thinking has to move closer to today as well. Otherwise, everything is always a course correction, and nothing ever really lands.
