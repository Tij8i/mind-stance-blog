The question that’s been going through my mind this morning is about the essence of reality: **what is real?**

More specifically, when it comes to the human experience and to our perception of abundance, what actually makes us live in a way that reflects abundance? Is it the concrete things we have, or is it the sense of what feels attainable to us?

I increasingly believe it is the latter. What we believe is attainable in the future has a far greater impact on our well-being than what we currently possess.

In economics, this idea already exists. Modigliani, I believe, won a Nobel Prize for formalizing the concept of expected wealth. The insight is simple but profound: people do not base their spending on what they earn today, but on what they expect to earn over time. Expectations, not current income, explain behavior better. In other words, what is “real” in the present matters less than what feels real about the future.

What struck me is how closely this maps onto our psychological life.

If we define well-being as the ability to enjoy the present while remaining at ease about what lies ahead, then it becomes clear that well-being is far more dependent on expectations than on circumstances. Two people can live in objectively similar conditions and experience life in radically different ways, simply because one sees a future they can buy into, while the other does not.

If I have many problems but I can clearly see their solutions or at least believe those solutions exist, I tend to feel grounded, even optimistic. The same problems, when perceived as permanent or unsolvable, quickly become overwhelming. The difference is not in the problems themselves, but in how the future is represented in the mind.

This brings me to something important: belief is not the same as blind optimism.

There is a difference between fooling oneself and holding a belief that feels earned. A believable future is not one where everything magically works out, but one where movement is possible. Where effort feels meaningful. Where the path forward may be uncertain, but not closed.

This is where expectation turns into something more subtle than hope. It becomes orientation.

From this angle, the mind appears less as a passive observer of reality and more as a prediction engine. We are constantly projecting forward, simulating outcomes, anticipating threats, and opportunities. Our experience of the present is deeply shaped by these projections. We don’t just live in reality — we live inside our interpretation of where reality is going.

Seen this way, the idea that “inner reality matters more than external reality” is not philosophical fluff. It’s practical. Our inner reality determines what feels consequential and what does not.

And this might explain something broader.

How much of human activity is driven by the need to stabilize the future? How much of science, progress, art, religion, and knowledge is fueled by the urge to reduce uncertainty, to make existence feel navigable? At their core, many of these endeavors are attempts to answer the same quiet question: “Will things be okay?”

In that sense, belief systems are not primarily about truth or falsehood. They are about livability.

And yet, when it comes to our own lives, we often forget this. We become overly focused on immediate problems, concrete goals, and measurable outcomes. We act as if solving the next issue will automatically restore our sense of ease.

I am not saying that goals are unimportant or that problems should be ignored. What I am saying is that without a grounded relationship to the future, problem-solving becomes frantic, and goals become heavy. Action without orientation drains us.

So perhaps the first task is not to fix anything at all, but to tune the mind toward a future that feels believable.

This is where choice enters the picture. Belief, in this sense, is a matter of choice, but not a casual one. It requires will. It requires the willingness to stand behind a perspective even when it is not fully supported by evidence yet. It also requires honesty, because the mind resists beliefs that feel fabricated.

When belief is chosen with integrity, it stops feeling like self-deception and becomes a commitment.

This also reframes aging in an interesting way.

As we grow older, life often feels heavier, not only because of physical decline, health issues, or social constraints, but because our sense of the future begins to shrink. What truly weighs us down is the feeling that things will not improve, that time no longer works in our favor.

But aging does not have to mean losing the future.

It could mean developing a stronger capacity to imagine beyond immediate outcomes, to believe in things we cannot yet see, whether that takes the form of spirituality, meaning, legacy, or even the idea of an afterlife. Alternatively, it could mean releasing the future altogether and learning to live day by day, grounded in an ego shaped over a lifetime, with quiet confidence and less need for validation.

Both paths, in their own way, restore grounding.

Which brings me back to the original question: what is more real, what surrounds us, or what lives in our mind?

Perhaps “real” is the wrong word. So is “abstract.” A more useful distinction might be consequential versus inconsequential. What shapes how we act, how we feel, how we show up, and what does not.

From that perspective, our inner representations of the future are not secondary. They are central. They are among the most consequential forces in our lives.

And maybe abundance, at its core, is not about accumulation at all, but about access to possibility.
