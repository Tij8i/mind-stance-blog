*In this post, I explore how true focus is not tunnel vision. Drawing from my experience in Aikido and the game of chess, I reflect on the paradox that real focus requires situational awareness. Peripheral vision is not a distraction from purpose, it is the foundation of strategy.*

Focus without awareness is blindness. This is one of the things Aikido taught me.

You learn to stay with your partner. 100% attention. But if you only watch your partner, you’ll miss the rest. You’ll get hit from the side. You won’t even see it coming.

That’s why in Aikido, one of the first things you train is peripheral vision. It might sound like a technical detail. It’s not. It changes your whole posture.

To maintain peripheral vision, your spine must be upright. Your hips open. Shoulders relaxed and wide. Head up, eyes soft. The body straightens into a martial posture: open yet ready. Proud, grounded. Present.

This posture isn’t for show. It’s not just aesthetic. It’s how your body aligns to make room for awareness. The alignment creates the mindset.

There is a training exercise called *Randori*, where you face multiple attackers coming at you from all sides. The goal is to move through them, redirecting each to the side, ensuring you don't get surrounded or cornered. At first glance, it seems chaotic. But the true function of the exercise is deeper: it teaches vision.

You learn to see without fixating. You learn to scan while staying present. To stay with your immediate challenge, while feeling the presence of everything around. This is not a contradiction. This is mastery.

And it teaches a bigger truth:

You cannot give 100% attention to something unless you are also aware of how it fits into the bigger picture.

Focus doesn’t exclude context. It requires it.

This brought to mind another place where I learned the same lesson: the chessboard.

Chess is often thought of as a game of calculation. But it is a game of vision. You can memorize openings. You can calculate lines. But what separates a good player from a great one is the ability to *see*.

Seeing, in chess, means understanding the field. Having a plan. Grasping how each piece might be useful, not just for the next move, but four moves down the line. It’s not about making moves. It’s about making meaning.

I love it when I place a piece for one reason, and five moves later, it turns out to serve a completely different purpose. That’s not just satisfying. That’s strategy.

Even more so when an opponent surprises me. There’s that moment of recognition: Ah, so that’s what the position meant.

That recognition is not luck. It comes from seeing the whole board, not just the next two squares.

It’s like peripheral vision in Aikido: you see more than what you’re directly engaging with. And from that place, you can adjust. Redeploy. Redirect.

It’s not about predicting. It’s about being aware enough to respond.

So what does this have to do with anything off the mat or off the board?

Everything.

We’re constantly told to develop focus. And that’s good. Mental stamina matters. The ability to work on something deeply, for hours, is a skill most people lack.

But here’s the thing: if we train focus without training vision, we’re just building a better tunnel.

We might succeed in one area. We might win a round. But we’ll lose the war.

A narrow focus, when not anchored in broader awareness, leads to what you might call *tactical wins in a losing game*.

You might be managing your day perfectly while ignoring the fact that the direction is off.

You might be solving problems that don’t matter.

You might be blocking attacks, unaware that five more are coming.

In strategy—in life—focus without peripheral vision is fatal.

There’s a mindset many people hold. A practical one:

> "Life is short. Meaning is slippery. Let’s get what we can while we can. Don’t overthink it."

There’s truth in that. And it can lead to a kind of street-smart clarity. It's survival. It's the hustle mindset.

But if your goal is to feel grounded, to lead a centered life, that mindset won’t take you there. Hustle doesn’t see the whole board. It reacts. It doesn’t respond.

To feel centered, to feel in control, your focus must *include* awareness.

It’s not focus *versus* vision. It’s focus *through* vision.
