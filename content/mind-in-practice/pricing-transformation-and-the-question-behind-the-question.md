*In this post, I explore how we think about the price of personal development products, and why pricing in transformation work functions as a mirror of our own beliefs and commitment.*

People sometimes ask me whether the personal development products I work with are “worth their price.” It’s an understandable question, and in the beginning I used to answer it directly. I would talk about the tools, the structure, the guidance, the outcomes people experienced. But over time the question started to feel incomplete. It was pointing to something else, something underneath the surface. So I began to sit with it. And eventually it triggered a broader reflection: how do we even price transformation? What makes a product “worth it”? And who is actually responsible for that value?

I think we all agree that meaningful change in our life is priceless. When you experience a shift in how you think, how you show up, how you act in the world, that shift has no obvious equivalent in euros or dollars. Yet, we still attach a price to the programs, tools, courses, and coaching that support these changes. And when we do that, we imply that transformation has a market value. We decide, consciously or not, that one kind of change is worth €50, another kind is worth €5000, and another perhaps €50,000. None of this is straightforward. None of it is neutral. And yet we treat it as if there is a logical, almost scientific way of determining what things should cost.

There isn’t. At least not across all categories of products.

Pricing sits on a spectrum. On one end, we have products whose value is mostly objective: fuel, utilities, raw goods, anything where cost of production, transport, regulation and competition shape the price in relatively predictable ways. On the other end, we have products where the value is mostly subjective: coaching, education, therapy, personal growth work, anything where the transformation depends primarily on the client, not the provider. These are two very different worlds, and yet we tend to judge them with the same vocabulary. No wonder the conversation gets confusing.

When someone buys petrol, they rarely question whether the petrol will “work.” They know what they’re paying for: a liquid that burns in the engine and pushes their car forward. The relationship between price and function is direct. But when someone buys a coaching program or a personal development tool, the same clarity is not available. The product does not “work” unless the person receiving it actually does something with it. It’s not a fuel you pour into your system. It’s more like an invitation, a structure, a mirror, a catalyst. And catalysts do nothing if the organism refuses to respond.

This is why pricing in the personal development space behaves differently.

It doesn’t just reflect the cost of what is being provided. It reflects the client’s expectations, their level of commitment, their readiness to change, and the psychological energy they are willing to invest. In many ways the price is part of the product itself. It’s not something you add on at the end; it’s something that helps shape the experience.

I’ve seen this repeatedly. A €50 program might contain excellent material, but if someone pays €50, they treat it as a minor experiment. If life gets busy, they drop it. If it gets hard, they avoid it. The signal they send themselves is: “This is nice to have, not essential.” On the other hand, a €5000 program can contain similar content, and yet the person will show up with a different posture: they carve out time, they stay present, they confront their own resistance, they stay in the process long enough for something real to shift. The price changes how they relate to the work.

Is this ethical? Some people question that. But before ethics, there is a psychological fact: commitment changes outcomes, and price is one of the strongest commitment mechanisms humans respond to.

#### Here is the more uncomfortable observation:

When someone says they are unwilling to invest in themselves — €1k, €5k, €10k — the hesitation almost never comes from the actual price. It comes from a lack of belief in their own ability to transform. And because that is hard to admit, especially to ourselves, we redirect the blame. We say we doubt the product. We say we don’t trust the program. We say it might not work. But the “it” in that statement is rarely the program. It’s us.

This is not a criticism. It’s just how the mind protects itself. If I don’t believe I can change, then investing in a tool that requires my active participation naturally feels risky. So instead of confronting that, I frame the hesitation as a rational evaluation of the product. It feels safer.

This pattern isn’t unique to coaching. I’ve been saying it for years in the education space: no one can teach you a language. They can guide you, support you, and provide structure, but the only person who can make you speak is you. If you’re not ready to take responsibility for the learning, the best teacher in the world can’t save you. And if you *are* ready, you can learn even with mediocre tools.

Personal development works exactly the same way. The product is not the transformation. The buyer is.

Once this is understood, the conversation about price changes completely. The real question is no longer, “Is this offer worth the money?” but rather, “Is this the right journey for me? Do I believe that if I step into this container, I will make something meaningful out of it?”

Price becomes a personal threshold, something that helps you decide whether you are ready to commit to the process. It’s not simply a calculation of value; it’s a check-in with your own agency.

This does not mean all prices are justified. There are products in the world that are priced unethically for all kinds of reasons: exploitation, exclusion, monopolistic behaviour, environmental damage, and artificially inflated perceived value. Those categories exist, and we should be aware of them. But when we talk specifically about coaching and personal development,  fields where the value is primarily psychological, a different dynamic applies. Here, pricing is less about the provider extracting value and more about the client creating it.

In this context, the price becomes a kind of doorway. Not everyone wants to walk through it, and not everyone needs to. But for those who do, the doorway itself plays a role in the transformation. It marks the moment where someone stops being a passive recipient of an “offer” and steps into being an active participant in their own process.

I’ve noticed this shift in myself too. When I look at a transformative product today, I don’t judge it by the euros alone. I ask whether the experience it promises resonates with the direction I’m trying to grow toward. Sometimes the answer is yes, sometimes no. But when it is yes, the price is simply the cost of crossing the threshold. Once I’ve crossed it, the responsibility is mine. I decide whether I make it worth my while.

This is why I think the conversation around pricing in personal development is so personal. It forces us to confront our beliefs about our own potential. It reflects how much we trust ourselves to follow through. It exposes the gap between what we say we want and what we are actually willing to commit to. And sometimes, the discomfort we feel around price is simply the discomfort of facing that gap.

So when someone asks me whether a product is “worth it,” the honest answer is that I don’t know. Not because the product is unclear, but because the real variable is the person asking. The product is a tool; the person is the engine. If the engine is ready, even a modest tool can create extraordinary change. If the engine isn’t ready, no price,  high or low, will make the difference.

In the end, pricing in personal development is not just about value delivered. It’s also about identity, belief, and readiness. It’s about stepping into a different relationship with your own agency. It’s about recognising that transformation is never something you buy; it’s something you choose to create. And once you take ownership of that, price becomes less about the product and more about the role you intend for it to play in your journey.

That’s why the question behind the question matters more than the answer.
