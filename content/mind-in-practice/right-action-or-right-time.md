*In this post I reflect on the tension between taking action and waiting for the right moment, and how organizational life subtly trains us into a form of strategic patience that can slowly pull us away from our own direction.*

There’s a fairly common idea in personal development that when something doesn’t work, you change it. You look at the situation as it is, notice what’s off, and do something different. New inputs, new results. It’s a mindset rooted in agency, in presence, in the belief that reality is something you can engage with rather than simply endure.

In many situations, that way of thinking is not only helpful, it’s necessary.

And yet, it sits in an uneasy relationship with how things actually work inside organizations.

Anyone who has spent time inside a large organization learns, often quite quickly, that timing matters just as much as correctness. Sometimes more. There are moments where nothing is fundamentally wrong, yet nothing moves. The idea is sound, the reasoning holds, but the environment isn’t ready. Or the political context isn’t aligned. Or the system simply hasn’t reached the point where change can stick.

So you wait.

You read the situation, you observe the pace of the organization, you sense where energy is flowing and where it’s blocked. You hold back, not because you lack clarity, but because acting now would be wasted effort. And very often, if you wait long enough, things do shift. People leave. Priorities change. Structures get rearranged. Suddenly what was impossible becomes obvious.

This kind of strategic patience is not passivity. It’s a real skill. In fact, it’s one of the core competencies you develop if you want to survive and progress inside complex systems. You learn how to synchronize yourself with something larger than you, something that doesn’t move at your personal rhythm.

The difficulty is that this skill doesn’t blend easily with the proactive mindset we often celebrate when we talk about growth or change.

Because once waiting becomes the default response, the line between discernment and avoidance starts to blur.

When you look at the present and recognize that something isn’t working, choosing not to act can be a legitimate decision. Context may change. External conditions may resolve the issue on their own. But psychologically, repeating that decision again and again has consequences. Over time, “I’m waiting for the right moment” can quietly turn into “I’m not moving.”

At that point, decision-making becomes strangely hollow. Everything is provisional. Everything depends on forces outside you. Action is always deferred to a future alignment that may or may not arrive.

What slowly shifts is your internal reference point.

Instead of asking what you want, or where you’re trying to go, you start asking whether your reading of reality is accurate enough. Whether your timing is right. Whether you’re being patient rather than impulsive. Your attention turns outward, toward the system, the environment, the constraints, and less toward your own direction.

Large organizations amplify this effect. The bigger the system, the easier it is to feel that most meaningful movement happens somewhere else, beyond your reach. Decisions are made upstream. Momentum builds elsewhere. You begin to experience yourself as someone who must wait for signals rather than generate them.

From the outside, this looks like realism. From the inside, it often feels like stagnation.

I’ve seen people wait for years this way. Highly capable people. Thoughtful, perceptive, strategically aware. Always ready, always observing, always one step away from acting. And with time, frustration sets in. Not because nothing ever happens, but because what happens rarely moves them closer to where they actually want to be.

I’ve lived this tension myself.

It’s also one of the reasons why moving from organizational life to working on your own is so psychologically demanding. The shift is not just practical, it’s deeply mental. When you work independently, there is no external rhythm to hide behind. No system whose timing you can blame or respect. If things don’t move, it’s rarely because the moment isn’t right. It’s because you’re not moving them.

At some point, you have to decide that now is the moment, even if conditions are imperfect, even if your standards aren’t fully met, even if what you’re producing feels rough compared to what you were trained to deliver inside an organization.

That’s another uncomfortable transition. Inside organizations, professionalism is often defined by polish, by adherence to process, by meeting externally imposed standards. Outside of that context, those same standards can become a form of inertia. They delay action. They keep you waiting for a version of readiness that may never fully materialize.

When you work on your own, direction matters more than refinement. Momentum matters more than elegance. Staying aligned with where you’re going matters more than how respectable each step looks along the way.

This doesn’t mean abandoning standards altogether. It means no longer letting them decide whether you move.

For me, this conflict was always present. Even when I understood, intellectually, why waiting made sense inside organizations, I never fully absorbed it as a mindset. Every time I had to suppress the impulse to act on something that felt clearly misaligned for me, there was a cost. A small disengagement. A subtle distancing from the mission, from the meaning of the work, from myself.

Toward the end, in certain situations, I did learn to adapt. To wait. To accept that pushing against reality wasn’t worth the effort. But that adaptation never felt neutral. It felt like a compromise, and with each compromise, work became less about direction and more about maintenance.

That’s when it turns into “just a job.”

And there’s nothing trivial about that. There’s money, stability, social legitimacy, a certain ease of life. All of it is real, and all of it has value. But psychologically, something shifts beneath the surface. You begin drifting, slowly, away from what you actually want, not through dramatic decisions, but through reasonable postponements.

You become very good at reading reality, and much less practiced at shaping it.

I don’t think there’s a clean formula to resolve this tension between waiting and acting. Both have their place. The difficulty is that one of them is safer, more socially reinforced, and more easily justified, especially inside systems. The other requires a tolerance for friction, imperfection, and uncertainty that most environments quietly discourage.

The real question, for me at least, is not whether waiting or acting is right. It’s whether waiting is still serving your direction, or whether it has quietly replaced it.

That distinction is subtle. And missing it is easier than we like to admit.
