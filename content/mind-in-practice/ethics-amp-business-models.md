*In this post, I explore what “ethics in business” really means, why the answer is never absolute, and where my current business model fits into that landscape.*

I’m often asked whether the business I work with is an MLM. It’s a fair question. When I first came across it, I had the same doubt. And it’s equally fair that people raise an eyebrow, because MLMs carry a heavy stigma. Some of that stigma is earned, some exaggerated, and some projected. But behind the question lies something deeper: a concern about ethics.

So I want to write about ethics, business models, and why, no matter how cleanly we try to cut the topic, it always frays at the edges.

When you look closely enough, no business is 100% ethical. Nothing we do is.

Action itself has a moral shadow. The moment you act, you create consequences, and consequences have ripple effects you don’t control. Hannah Arendt wrote about this, noting that every human action enters a web of relationships that expands beyond its intention. Religion saw this long before philosophy gave it a name. That’s partly why so many traditions hold contemplation above action. Non-action is the only place where nothing can go wrong, because nothing happens. The moment you step into the world, you disturb it in ways you can’t fully foresee.

So the question isn’t whether something is ethical or unethical.

The question is: *to what degree*, *on which levels*, and *according to whom*?

The ethics of selling popcorn at a cinema, running a tech startup, working in finance, or performing heart surgery are fundamentally different. Not in intention. Not in effect. Not in perception.

You can’t compare a scammer and a doctor. But it’s also true that even doctors, with all their training and noble intent, sometimes impose their own values on patients “for their own good.” That’s when good intentions are still ethically complicated. And it happens in every profession: we project our values, we justify our decisions, we do what makes sense to us, and we call it moral.

So when people ask, “Is your business ethical?”, I’m tempted to say: compared to what? According to whose scale? And measured in which dimension?

Ethics in business is never about perfection. It’s about alignment.

There’s an internal layer and an external one.

Internally, the question is:

*Does what I do feel aligned with who I want to be? Does it add something to the world rather than subtract?*

Externally, the question is:

*Would others feel misled, harmed, or disappointed if they saw the full picture of how this business works? Or would they be comfortable, maybe even enthusiastic, knowing everything?*

That’s the real test. Not an abstract moral code, but the emotional reaction of the people who interact with the product and the model.

But before we go there, we need to admit something uncomfortable. Most of humanity operates with at least some degree of selfishness. Virtue exists, but it coexists with self-interest. If you ask someone why they do what they do, they will tell a story that flatters their intention. We all do. Our psychology is built around protecting the narrative of our own goodness.

And yet, this doesn’t make us unethical. It makes us human.

Even in Buddhism, a tradition obsessed with reducing harm, there is an understanding that purity is impossible while living in the world of form. You breathe, you cause harm. You walk, you disturb the earth. You work, you trade influence. The goal isn’t purity. It’s awareness.

So when we talk about ethics in business, the conversation should not be about absolutes. It should be about understanding the trade-offs we’re choosing.

This brings me to MLMs and why they are so despised.

I’m not an MLM evangelist. I don’t think they’re all evil either. I simply think the moral discomfort they trigger has specific structural reasons.

MLMs, by design, create a power-law distribution. A few people earn a lot. Many earn little or nothing. The same is true in many industries—tech startups, real estate, finance, but in MLMs the spread is sharper, and the communication around it is often poorly managed.

People are promised transformations that rarely materialize. Expectations are inflated beyond what the model can support. And the distance between what is said and what is likely creates an ethical tension that people feel in their bones.

That’s why MLMs get the reputation they get. Not necessarily because the model is inherently immoral, but because the gap between potential and probability is rarely communicated with full honesty. And when people feel misled, they attack the model rather than the communication.

That said, it’s also true that some people choose MLMs consciously.

Not because they’re naïve, but because they enjoy the challenge.

They look at the structure and think: *Let me see if I can rise to the top. Let me bet on myself. Let me try to be the exception.*

There’s nothing unethical about that. It’s a personal trial. A mountain to climb. And failure doesn’t make the attempt immoral. It just makes it human.

So where does my business model fit into all this?

The business I work with is not MLM. It doesn’t rely on extreme income differences built on downline performance. It doesn’t require aggressive quotas. It doesn’t promise unrealistic upside.

What it *does* share with MLM-like models is the idea that if you want a different life, you cannot do what everyone else is doing. Not because you’re better than others, but because you’re choosing a different trajectory. You are willing to stand apart from the crowd—not socially, but mentally.

To think differently, to move differently, to take responsibility for your own direction.

This is where many people feel discomfort.

Not because the model is unethical, but because the mindset it requires is unfamiliar. It asks you to question your limits. It asks you to look beyond conventional safety. And if someone isn’t ready for that, the whole thing can look suspicious or reckless or morally ambiguous.

Right decision, right time.

Five years ago, this wouldn’t have been right for me either. I wasn’t in the mindset for it. Today I am. And someone else might be exactly where I was then.

What matters is that our decisions make sense to us.

Because the truth is that society has become obsessed with derisking life. We build systems to avoid uncertainty. We use “ethics” as justification to avoid anything that might push us into responsibility for ourselves.

And so entire categories of business get painted as dangerous, not because they are structurally immoral, but because they trigger unfamiliar levels of personal agency.

Talking about ethics is important. But sometimes it’s a shield people hide behind so they don’t have to confront the question: *Is this too big for me? Or am I simply not ready to bet on myself yet?*

That’s why, for me, the most honest way to talk about ethics in business is this:

- Know what you’re entering.

- Understand the trade-offs.

- Be clear with yourself about why you’re doing it.

- Don’t outsource your judgment to public opinion.

What I do now aligns with my values, my ambitions, and my desire to see people take themselves seriously—even if it’s uncomfortable, even if it’s risky.

But I don’t ask anyone to adopt my view. I don’t think this model should appeal to everyone. It’s for people who feel the pull to step out of the predictable path and see what they’re capable of.

If that idea irritates you or feels too much, absolutely fine. It’s simply not your moment. If it excites you, then we’re probably speaking the same language.

Ethics in business is never a clean, polished subject. It’s messy, human, and it always comes down to what you can justify to yourself when no one is watching.
