This morning I thought about that quote often attributed to Mark Twain: *“I’ve had thousands of problems in my life, most of which never actually happened.”*

At first sight my instinct is to push back. Because yes, most problems never happen—but isn’t it also true that they don’t happen precisely because we have thought of them? Isn’t it because we planned, prepared, stayed alert, that we avoided them in the first place? So why dismiss thinking ahead?

But then the quote stays with me. It shifts once I focus not on whether problems materialize, but on the word itself: *problems.*

It is one thing to anticipate scenarios and prepare for them. It is something else entirely to live as if those scenarios are already present problems. Worry blurs that line. The mind takes what might happen and translates it into something heavy, something real now.

This is where the quote lands for me: it’s not a call to stop planning, it’s a reminder to stop carrying hypothetical problems as if they already exist.

Because how many of us live like that? How many choices—job, family, lifestyle—are driven by the fear of being broke, lonely, or sad in the future? How many of us measure our present by what dangers it avoids later?

We are taught from childhood to think ahead. Aesop’s tale of the ant and the cricket. School lessons about saving, planning, sacrificing instant pleasure for future gains. At the same time, folk wisdom counters with sayings like “better an egg today than a chicken tomorrow.” A balance exists, though we rarely see it.

Still, when we think of success, we almost always equate it with foresight. The clever ones are those who map the future, who anticipate pitfalls, who prepare. Rarely do we call successful the one who simply keeps a clear mind, who worries less, who focuses on what is actually here.

And yet—that may be the real art. To keep the mind from manufacturing troubles. To notice the difference between planning and worrying. To live without dragging possible futures into the present.

It sounds simple. It isn’t easy. But it’s a stance I want to keep practicing: worry less, live now.
