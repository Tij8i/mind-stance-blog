*In this post I explore why the wisdom of making decisions right, rather than endlessly evaluating them, anchors us in freedom, conviction, and real growth.*

### **My Intuition**

*Sometimes you make the right decision; sometimes you make the decision right.*

This line has been circling my mind for a while. It’s not just a clever play on words—it’s a compass. It cuts through hesitation and points straight to the heart of action.

### **My Reasoning**

We live surrounded by quotes. Social media is full of them, books are peppered with them, podcasts recite them like mantras. Most quotes stay at the level of inspiration: they sound nice, they make you nod, but then they dissolve. A few, though, go deeper. They stick, because you make them yours. You don’t just understand them; you feel them in your belly when life presses. This is one of those quotes for me.

“Sometimes you make the right decision, sometimes you make the decision right.” The origin is often traced to Phil McGraw (Dr. Phil), though Ellen Langer, a Harvard psychologist, has written a close variation: *“Don’t make the right decision; make the decision right.”* The essence is the same: stop hunting for the perfect choice and start living the choice you made.

#### **A corollary to Yoda**

I see this as a corollary to Yoda’s famous line: *“Do or do not. There is no try.”* Both point to the same truth: conviction is what breathes life into decisions. Without conviction, a decision is just a mental exercise.

We spend hours—sometimes years—trying to forecast outcomes. We compare scenarios, analyze angles, consult endless experts. All of this has its place. But at some point, analysis turns into paralysis. And when it does, the decision is no longer a doorway; it becomes a prison.

What the quote reminds me is this: the decisive factor in life is not whether we make the *best* decision, but whether we *stand by it with presence and energy.*

#### **What happens after a “wrong” decision**

We all know the feeling of making a choice and then doubting it. You sign a contract, accept a job, start a relationship, invest time in a project—and soon after, your mind starts replaying: “Was this smart? Did I overlook something? What if I had chosen differently?”

The doubts multiply. We start arguing with ourselves, imagining alternate realities. Instead of being in the experience, we hover above it, questioning our motives and intelligence. Energy drains not in the doing, but in the doubting.

This is when we sabotage ourselves. The step from doubting to trying is short, and the step from trying to quitting is even shorter. We all know how close those steps are.

This quote serves as a reminder:  “don’t waste your strength on counterfactuals. Use it to live the choice”

#### **The value of conviction**

Making the right decision is important, yes. But the critical point is not the decision itself—it’s the conviction that follows. Without conviction, the decision collapses under its own weight. With conviction, even a shaky path teaches us, strengthens us, grows us.

Mistakes are inevitable. But mistakes lived with full commitment carry insight. Mistakes half-lived carry only regret. To truly learn, we need to go all in, to experience fully what doesn’t work. That is how clarity emerges.

Think of an athlete. A golfer can’t learn balance by reading about it. He learns by swinging, missing, recalibrating, swinging again. The “wrong” swings are not wasted—they are the practice that builds precision. In life, the “wrong” decisions are not failures; they are swings that teach us where our stance is off.

#### **The fear of judgment**

Another layer: often we are less concerned about the actual outcome of a decision than about how others will perceive it. We fear the judgment of being wrong, more than being wrong itself.

That’s why this quote is liberating. It doesn’t say: “Defend your choices against the world.” It says: “Live them without apology.” If the decision was yours to make, then it’s yours to embody.

This is not arrogance. It’s not about stubbornly insisting we were right when we were not. It’s about not outsourcing our freedom. It’s about not needing external justification for every step.

The distinction is crucial: making the decision right is not about proving it right to others. It’s about living it right for yourself.

#### **A counter-voice**

Of course, part of me resists this. The voice says: “But isn’t that dangerous? What if you keep pushing forward on a wrong path, blind to your mistakes? Doesn’t that turn into arrogance or hypocrisy?”

That’s a valid concern. And here is the nuance: making the decision right does not mean ignoring feedback or reality. It doesn’t mean stubbornly clinging to a sinking ship. It means committing enough to experience reality fully, to see clearly if it’s working or not, and then adjusting with dignity.

Arrogance is about defending choices to the bitter end. Freedom is about living choices fully without needing to defend them.

#### **The psychological research**

Psychologists distinguish between “maximizers” and “satisficers.” Maximizers are those who chase the absolute best choice. They analyze every option, weigh every possibility, and often end up dissatisfied, no matter what they pick. Satisficers, in contrast, decide based on sufficiency—“good enough”—and then move forward. Research shows satisficers are generally happier and less stressed.

This quote embodies the satisficer’s wisdom: stop agonizing over “the best,” and pour your energy into “making it best for you.”

#### **The inner leader**

When you live a decision with conviction, something shifts inside. You become your own leader. Others notice it too. Conviction is magnetic—it inspires, because it shows integrity in action.

This doesn’t mean you will always succeed. But even in failure, conviction inspires. It shows courage. It shows alignment between word and deed. That is leadership in its purest form.

#### **Freedom to fail, freedom to succeed**

Most of us know the fear of failing. Fewer of us recognize the fear of succeeding. Yet both fears are real. What if we try something, succeed, and it changes our life? What if others expect more of us after that? What if success isolates us?

The quote whispers: let go of both fears. Don’t fear failing. Don’t fear succeeding. Just decide, and then live it. As the Nike slogan goes: *Just do it.*

### **Conclusion**

Decisions are not monuments to perfection. They are invitations to life. The practice is not to choose flawlessly, but to live the choice fully.

Sometimes you make the right decision. Other times, you make the decision right.

Both paths lead forward. Both are practice. Both are freedom.
