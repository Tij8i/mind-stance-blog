*In life we don’t just choose opportunities, we choose the problems that come with them. What challenges are you willing to face to grow?*

I’ve always been taught to look for opportunities. My father was entrepreneurial in the truest sense—he saw possibility everywhere. He had an optimistic outlook, a confidence in himself and in life. I learned that habit too. It became my lens: stay open, look for the new, say yes.

That mindset has taken me far. I’ve lived in different countries, worked across industries, stepped into leadership, pursued paths that were anything but straight. Always chasing the next opening, the next possibility.

But here’s the truth I’ve learned: when you only chase opportunity, you end up shifting away whenever adversity shows up. The moment problems appear, the natural tendency is to pivot toward something “easier,” something fresh. I know this because I’ve done it—often.

At some point, though, you realize you can’t keep dodging problems. You have to pick them. The challenges you choose are what shape you. They are the weight you train with. They are what builds you.

So the question becomes: what problems do I want to face in my life?

This is a different lens than asking, “What’s the right decision?” or “Will this get me to my goal?” Because maybe the truth is: most paths will eventually get you there. Some will take longer. Some will test you differently. Some will demand patience, others courage. But all of them can get you to your destination.

So the real decision isn’t *where* you want to go. It’s *how* you want to get there. What kind of resistance do you want to wrestle with? What set of obstacles will you allow to shape you?

This is the shift: from “Is this right or wrong?” to “How do I want to grow along the way?”

Psychologists call this a growth mindset—the belief that we develop through effort and persistence, not through talent alone. Philosophers like Viktor Frankl took it further: we may not choose our suffering, but we can always choose our stance toward it. And in the entrepreneurial world, this is nothing new—every venture is less about finding a perfect idea than about finding problems you’re willing to solve, day in and day out.

Once you see this, decisions take on a new clarity. You stop obsessing about whether you’re choosing the “right” path. You trust that whichever path you choose, it will bring results, because you will keep walking. The only real choice is: what kind of problems do you want to engage with?

That shift alone brings energy. Confidence. Curiosity. Because when you ask *how* things can get better, instead of *whether* they can, you’ve already put yourself in motion. You’ve left the safety of your head and stepped into action.

So here’s the reflection I leave you with today:

- What are the problems I have already chosen in my life?

- Which ones are worth continuing to face?

- And what new challenges do I want to commit to, knowing they will shape me into who I want to become?

The opportunity and the adversity are inseparable. To choose one is to choose the other
