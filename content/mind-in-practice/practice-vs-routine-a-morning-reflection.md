Two quotes have been echoing in my mind this morning:

> ### *“You don’t have to control your thoughts. You just have to stop letting them control you.”* — Dan Millman

> ### *“Knowing is not enough, we must apply.”* — Bruce Lee

I shared both recently, and today they feel particularly alive. They speak to something I’ve been wrestling with quietly: the fine line between *practice* and *routine*. On the surface, they can look the same. But underneath, there’s a fundamental difference and that difference is awareness.

Yesterday I had a conversation that nudged me to confront something uncomfortable. The idea that *practice*, which I often associate with presence, growth, and intention, can subtly morph into *routine*, a comforting disguise for inertia. In other words, routine is practice stripped of awareness. It’s going through the motions, driven not by what matters deeply to me, but by what my mind insists must be done.

That hit home.

I’ve been procrastinating on a task for over a week. And it’s not because I’ve been lazy. On the contrary, I’ve been busy, full of good reasons and high-priority to-dos. But underneath, there’s been a persistent voice in my head whispering: *“That task doesn’t align with your path. You need more clarity first. You need a better plan. You’re not ready yet.”*

So I listened. I let the voice steer me. I wrapped that procrastination in good intentions and called it thoughtful. But really, I just let my thoughts control me. That’s Millman’s point, isn’t it?

Now here’s Bruce Lee chiming in: *Knowing is not enough.*

I *know* I’ve been avoiding. I *know* I’ve let routine sneak in under the guise of discernment.

The next question is the hard one:

What am I going to do about it?

Seeing the trap is not enough. Understanding the difference between practice and routine is not enough. The shift happens only when I act.

Otherwise, I’ll keep walking the same path — thinking about change, talking about change — but not actually changing.

And that, to me, is the discipline of real practice:

To listen, to observe, and then to move even when the mind offers convincing reasons not to.
