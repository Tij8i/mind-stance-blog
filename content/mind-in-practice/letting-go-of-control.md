I had a conversation the other day with a friend who builds software. We were talking about how AI is changing the way we code, and how that shift is starting to mess with our assumptions—not just about work, but about control.

He said something that stuck with me:

> **“I don’t really control my codebase anymore. I supervise it.”**

That hit a nerve. Because it’s not just about code. It’s about how so many of us are still wired to approach life and work as if they were controllable systems, predictable, deterministic, engineer-able. And that mindset is breaking. Quietly, but definitely.

In the past, I think I relied a lot on the idea that if I just worked hard enough, prepared well enough, kept my hands on the wheel, I could more or less make things go where I wanted. It was comforting. And, to some extent, it worked. But that model only works when the environment is relatively stable. When variables don’t change too quickly. When the logic is consistent.

But now? I don’t know. Everything feels more fluid. Systems adapt faster than we can anticipate. The outcomes don’t always follow from the inputs. And the harder I try to hold on to control things in the old way, the more tension I feel. The less natural it all becomes.

There’s something fundamentally different about designing for systems you no longer fully understand. Where the outcome is a *result* of your input, but not entirely caused by it. That’s the reality with AI. And maybe also with people. With life. With relationships. And definitely with parenting.

So I’ve been sitting with that: the idea that my job might no longer be to control, but to participate. To sense. To adjust. To guide lightly instead of gripping tightly.

It’s not easy. My default is still to reach for structure. To find the plan. To make the checklist and feel like I’ve accounted for everything. But the more I do that, the more I see its limitations. There’s always something I missed. Something I couldn’t have predicted. Some dependency I didn’t even know existed.

I used to think success came from being ahead of the curve. From having it all mapped out. But now I’m starting to believe that real strength comes from how well I can stay grounded when things don’t go to plan. When I’m in the middle of the fog and there’s no clean signal.

This brings up the distinction I’ve been reflecting on more clearly lately, the difference between external control and internal control.

External control is what most of us were trained to value: getting outcomes, managing people, setting up systems that work exactly as intended. But more and more, I find myself shifting toward internal control: managing my response, holding my emotional center, noticing when I’m triggered and choosing not to react in the same old way.

Because the outside world doesn’t really respond to tight control anymore. And I don’t think it ever really did—I was just better at pretending it did.

AI is just a mirror. It’s making that visible. You can prompt it, guide it, course correct, but you don’t *command* it. You don’t fully control what it gives you. And the work becomes about staying in relationship with the process, not dominating it.

That feels like a bigger lesson. One I’m trying to integrate.

What can I actually control?

My breathing. My tone of voice. My attention. My posture. The clarity of my intention.

Not much else, really.

Not how people will receive me.

Not how fast something will grow.

Not whether someone will understand what I meant.

Not whether my plans will unfold in the right order.

It’s unsettling at first. But it’s also freeing.

Because the energy I used to spend trying to force or predict or over-engineer… I can now put toward staying present. Not giving up. Just being more honest about where my leverage really is.

And interestingly, things often move better that way. Not when I grip harder, but when I show up with presence and let the system move with me. Like working with current, instead of against it.

I’m also noticing this shift in how I lead projects. In the past, I thought my job was to make sure nothing slipped through the cracks. To carry everything in my head. To be the net. Now I’m trying to build with more trust, more breathing room, more resilience to unpredictability. Because that’s where we are now—things are uncertain by default.

And if that’s true, then what matters most is: can I remain calm in the face of uncertainty? Can I act without full clarity? Can I still take responsibility for my part, without assuming I should be able to control the whole thing?

That’s what I’m training now. Letting go of the false belief that control means safety. And replacing it with something else:

That stability comes from the inside.

That clarity doesn’t have to mean certainty.

That influence isn’t the same as control.

It’s subtle, but it shifts everything.

I’m not trying to fix this like it’s a flaw. It’s more like reprogramming my relationship with effort. Still working. Still engaged. But from a different posture.

More open. Less rigid.

More listening. Less forcing.

More responding. Less assuming.

And weirdly, I feel more capable, not less. Because I’m no longer spending all that mental energy trying to hold up a system that was never really under my command to begin with.

Letting go doesn’t mean letting things fall apart.

It means recognizing what’s mine to hold. And what never was.
