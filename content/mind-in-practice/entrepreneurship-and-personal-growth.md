*In this post, I reflect on a powerful early experience during my MBA, and how it revealed something essential about entrepreneurship and personal development: there is a time to think, and a time to act. I speak about the trap of overthinking, the monkey mind, and why training a bias for action might be the highest form of self-work.*

There’s a moment I’ve never forgotten from the early days of my MBA. First entrepreneurship class. The professor walks in, says hello, and without warning hands out a challenge:

"You have 30 seconds. Draw as many circles as you can. Go."

The room froze.

What kind of circles? On what paper? With what pen? Should they overlap? Should they be perfect? Should we use the full page? Should we draw fast or draw well?

By the time the questions were halfway out of our mouths, the clock was up. Most had only drawn a few circles. Some had none. That was the point.

There’s a time to think. And there’s a time to just do.

That exercise stuck with me. It showed something simple but real: how easy it is to stall when things aren't clear. The hesitation. The need for more info. The fear of doing it wrong.

Entrepreneurship doesn’t wait for everything to line up. You move with what you’ve got. You figure things out as you go. You build, not because it's perfect, but because you're in motion.

And still, I forget this all the time.

I’m wired to learn, to analyze, to build frameworks. That’s how I make sense of things. But it's also how I avoid doing.

There’s a strange comfort in thinking. You feel like you're making progress. But unless you act, nothing really changes.

Action is uncomfortable. It’s exposing. It forces you to stand behind something—however rough or early it may be.

And yet, that’s where things start to shift.

This isn’t about glorifying hustle or pretending every move should be fast. It’s about recognizing those moments when you already know what the next step is, but you stall anyway.

Entrepreneurship brings those moments to the surface. It reflects back whether you act or delay.

And it’s the same with personal development.

The mind always finds reasons to wait. You call it research. Or preparation. Or being strategic. But often, it’s just fear in disguise.

That’s why Yoda’s old line lands so hard: “Do or do not. There is no try.”

Try is what we say when we want to keep our options open. When we don’t want to commit.

But life doesn’t care about ‘try.’ Either you sent the email or you didn’t. Either you built it or you didn’t. Either you acted or you didn’t.

Even Amazon—arguably one of the most structured organizations out there—has this idea baked into its leadership principles: "Have a bias for action."

It’s not about speed. It’s about not waiting forever. It’s about understanding that in the face of uncertainty, movement beats perfection.

That’s why I see entrepreneurship as a kind of personal practice. It teaches you what you actually do—not just what you believe or intend.

Because once you act, even in a small way, something becomes real. You’re no longer just thinking about change. You’re participating in it.

This isn’t wisdom from a mountaintop. It’s something I bump into again and again in my work, in my decisions, in my daily resistance to getting started.

Some days I still overthink. Some days I hold back. The monkey mind is alive and well. But I’m trying to catch it sooner. To move through the hesitation. To take that first step.

Because the goal isn’t to act perfectly. It’s to act honestly. Even a rough start is better than no start.

The circles don’t need to be perfect. They just need to be on the page.

**Conclusion:** If there’s something you care about—an idea, a project, a version of yourself you want to live into—at some point, you’ll have to act. Not because you’re sure. Not because it’s the perfect moment. But because action is the only way to move from thought to reality.

Even one small mark can change everything.

Make it.
