*In this post, I reflect on what success means, not the version sold to us by society, but the version that emerges when we stop playing other people’s games and start defining our own. This is about realignment, not rebellion. It’s about choosing your scoreboard and playing to win there.*

Success is an overinflated word. It’s been hijacked.

Say “success” and your mind will show you money, headlines, power suits, and stage lights.

That’s not an accident. It’s programming. It’s the product of a society built on growth, comparison, and categories.

I’m an economist. I understand why the system evolved this way. It has its logic. It has delivered value. But that’s not what this post is about. This is about reclaiming the word. Because if we don’t, we risk living a life optimized for someone else’s definition of winning.

**Here’s the real issue:**

Success, in its current form, is external.
It’s visible. It’s validated. It’s performative.

We know this. But we keep chasing it anyway.
Why? Because recognition matters.

There’s no shame in that. We’re social beings. Meaning lives in shared experience.

Even service, the most personal form of success, gains depth when it’s seen and acknowledged by others.

**But here’s the catch:**

That same drive for recognition is what distorts our compass.

It’s what makes us crave the *status* of the doctor, the entrepreneur, the CEO, without checking if the *function* of those roles aligns with our purpose.

We shortcut. We cosplay. We signal.

And sometimes, without realizing it, we become performers in someone else’s theater.

**It’s subtle, but it’s real.**

The feedback loop of recognition can be healthy when it confirms we’re being of service.

It turns toxic when we mistake the label for the work.

That’s when you get the “successful” executive manipulating numbers.

The “influential” politician is chasing power for power’s sake.

People who’ve lost the thread. Who’ve traded purpose for applause.

That’s not success.

That’s ego with a marketing budget.

**So what’s the alternative?**

Don’t reject recognition. Reposition it.

Let it be the echo of your purpose, not the source of your direction.

When we define success as alignment between what we do, who we are, and why we’re here, then recognition becomes a bonus, not a crutch.

Success, then, becomes internal.

It becomes personal.

And it becomes sustainable.

**How do you measure that?**

You measure it by fidelity to purpose.

You ask: Am I on track with *my* values?

Am I moving toward what *I* said matters?

And yes, this takes clarity. It takes practice.

One tool I’ve found helpful is visualization—not the vision board kind, but the internal kind. The kind that rewires your system from the inside out.

Dr. Jim Doty, Stanford neurosurgeon and author of *Into the Magic Shop*, has shown how visualization, when tied to intention, can literally reshape the brain.

He speaks of focused attention combined with heartfelt purpose.

That’s not wishful thinking. That’s neuroplasticity.

Visualization helps us *remember forward*, to project our truest selves into the future, and then live today in service of that version.

When done with sincerity, it’s not a productivity trick.

It’s a way of setting your compass before you take a step.

**And when the envy creeps in?**

Because it will. When someone else’s life looks shinier, cleaner, more admired.

Here’s the line I go back to:

Comparison debilitates.

It dilutes your focus.

It breaks your internal metric and replaces it with someone else’s scoreboard.

It tricks you into playing games you were never meant to win.

You lose not because you’re less capable, but because the rules were never written with you in mind.

*Don’t play losing games.*

Instead, measure yourself by your own roster.

Choose your game.

Track your progress.

Refine your aim.

And forget about shiny objects being thrown at you to distract, seduce, or manipulate you into changing course. That’s not alignment. That’s advertising.

### **🔍 For further exploration**

- Dr. Jim Doty – Into the Magic Shop: Visualization as a neuroscientific practice for personal transformation.

- Viktor Frankl – Man’s Search for Meaning: Success as a by-product of purpose, not a goal in itself.

- Naval Ravikant: “Play long-term games with long-term people.” Success without alignment isn’t real.

- Tara Brach: Talks extensively on the trance of unworthiness and how comparison feeds ego loops.

**Conclusion:**

Success isn’t a trophy.

It’s a signal.

It tells you when you’re living in alignment. When your actions match your values. When you’re playing *your* game, on *your* terms.

That’s the only scoreboard worth tracking.
