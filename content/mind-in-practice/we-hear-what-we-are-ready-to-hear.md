*Our attention is selective. What we hear, what we notice, is not the same as what is said.*

*In this post I reflect on why two people can listen to the same words yet walk away with entirely different understandings. Drawing from Aikido, neuroscience, and learning in sports, music, and language, I explore how our selective attention and inner models shape what we hear, and why it matters for growth.*

## **Intuition**

We hear what we need to hear.

Not what is said. Not even what we think is said. What we *are able* to hear in that moment, with our present filters, our current models of the world.

## **Reasoning**

### **Selective by Design**

The human brain cannot process everything. At every moment, millions of sensory signals are hitting us. Light, sound, movement, words, gestures. Our attention filters ruthlessly. It chooses what to amplify, what to let fade.

Psychologists call this selective attention. Classic experiments—like the “cocktail party effect”—show how we can follow one conversation in a noisy room and miss everything else. Broadbent proposed a strict “filter” model in the 1950s: attention acts like a gate, blocking most input. Later, Treisman suggested an “attenuation” model: unattended signals are not fully blocked but reduced in strength, waiting in the background. That is why if someone says your name across the room, you suddenly notice.

In short, our ears are open, but our mind decides what gets through.

### **Same Words, Different Meanings**

Think of advice from a teacher, trainer, or mentor. The words don’t change, but the *meaning you extract* evolves.

I see this clearly in Aikido.

From my very first class, my Sensei repeated: “Aikido is not about form. It is about blending, centering, perceiving.”

But as a beginner, I could not hear it. My selective attention was locked on hands and feet—*Where do I step? How do I turn?* His words floated over me. They left an imprint, yes, but I could not make sense of them.

Weeks later, after I learned the steps, I heard him say it again. This time, it was not about hands and feet but about *timing and distance.* I finally had ears for that. Later still, I realized timing and distance only make sense if my body is balanced, centered. The same advice—focus on centering—resurfaced at each stage, with new depth.

His words didn’t change. *My filters changed.* And each time, even without full understanding, the message moved me forward.

### **Modelling Reality**

This is the deeper mechanism. Our perception is not a raw recording. It is a model. The brain builds predictions about reality, then compares them against sensory input. This is the predictive processing view of the mind.

What we hear is filtered through these models. If my model of Aikido is “hands and feet,” then that is what I perceive in my teacher’s words. If my model evolves to “blending and balance,” then the same words suddenly reveal another layer.

In other words: *we don’t perceive reality as it is. We perceive what our models allow us to see.*

### **Learning Across Domains**

This is not unique to martial arts.

- Music: A beginner hears notes. An advanced musician hears harmony, texture, tension, release. The same piece of music, completely different experience.

- Language: A beginner hears strange sounds. Later, they hear words. Later still, they hear tone, irony, subtext. Same sentences, new levels.

- Sports: A novice tennis player hears “watch the ball” and focuses on eye contact. A pro hears the same phrase and interprets it as reading spin, anticipating bounce, positioning early.

In each case, *same words, different story*. The words are seeds. What grows depends on the soil of our experience.

### **The Trap of Judgment**

Here is the practical consequence: if what we hear is filtered, then judging words as “right” or “wrong” too quickly is a trap.

We close ourselves off when we say: *“That doesn’t make sense”* or *“That’s wrong.”* It might be that it doesn’t make sense *yet*. It might be that our current model cannot integrate it—but in time, with practice, it will.

Sometimes the wisest move is to suspend judgment. To let words sit. To ask: *What do these words mean for me, right now, in this stage of my journey?*

That shift, from demanding absolute correctness to seeking personal relevance, is where growth happens.

### **Neuroscience Behind the Shift**

Modern neuroscience supports this.

- Perceptual Load Theory (Nilli Lavie): under heavy cognitive load (learning basics), our filter is strict; we block out subtle cues. When load decreases (skills become automatic), more peripheral signals seep in.

- Predictive Processing (Friston, Clark): perception is prediction-driven; our models generate expectations, and what doesn’t fit is either discarded or forces the model to evolve.

- Attenuation Models (Treisman): even unattended signals leave traces; they are not wasted, just waiting until they resonate.

This is why feedback you don’t fully “get” still influences you. Why a quote you heard years ago suddenly “clicks” today. Why ancient wisdom can sound banal in youth, profound in age.

### **The Practice of Open Ears**

The practical lesson is simple, but not easy:

- Stop forcing full comprehension. You don’t need to grasp every nuance today.

- Notice what resonates now. That is your entry point, the part of the message your filters allow.

- Trust what will resurface. Words you don’t understand now may return when you are ready.

This is not passive listening. It is active openness. It is the discipline of keeping ears unclenched.

### **Aikido as a Metaphor for Attention**

In Aikido, if you are too rigid, you miss the flow. If you over-focus on one hand, you miss the whole body. The art demands wide perception: soft gaze, centered stance, openness.

Listening is the same. If we grip too tightly to *understanding* or *judging*, we miss the subtle signals. But if we relax into awareness, the message comes through, not all at once, but in layers.

### **Closing the Circle**

So we return to the starting thought:

We hear what we are ready to hear.

Words are not fixed packets of meaning. They are invitations. What we accept depends on where we stand, how we filter, what our models allow.

This is liberating. You don’t need to “get it all” now. You don’t need to decide immediately if words are right or wrong. You can take what resonates, let the rest sit.

Growth is not about hearing everything. It is about building the capacity to hear more.

Every practice, every lesson, every conversation, expands the ear.

## **Conclusion**

Listening is not about decoding words. It is about what your current self is able to perceive. Your models, your filters, your readiness.

The same sentence can mean something shallow today, profound tomorrow, and something else entirely ten years later.

So stop trying to “understand it all.” Stop judging too soon. Instead, practice open ears. Trust that what you hear today is enough—and that tomorrow, with practice, you will hear more.

That is growth. Not accumulating words, but refining perception. Not expanding what is said, but expanding what can be heard.
