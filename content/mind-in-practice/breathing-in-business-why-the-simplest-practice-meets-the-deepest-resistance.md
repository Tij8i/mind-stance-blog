In this post, I speak about how breathing—a seemingly basic human act—holds the power to change how we work, relate, and perform. I explore the psychological and cultural resistances that keep it out of our daily professional routines, and share what I’m doing to shift that in my own practice.

**Take a breath. Now hold it.**

That’s how most of us work.

We rush, speak, decide, and lead—all while barely breathing. And yet, we all say we’re mindful. We know it’s good for us. We’ve read the articles, maybe even downloaded a meditation app. We claim awareness, but often we’re just aware that we should be more aware.

I say this with no judgment. I’m the same. I know what conscious breathing can do. And yet I often forget to do it. In today’s post, I want to reflect on why something so simple, accessible, and well-documented still meets such strong resistance—and what I’ve been doing to turn that around.

**First, the obvious.**

I won’t get into how breathing supports mindfulness or list all the business benefits. Those have been covered endlessly: better public speaking, more centered decision-making, conflict resolution, team cohesion, lower stress levels. The studies are there, the books are out. This is not a knowledge problem.

It’s not a capability problem either. Breathing doesn’t require expensive gear or elite coaching. You just need presence and repetition.

And yet… most don’t do it.

**So why don’t we breathe?**

Three levels of resistance, each one deeper than the last.

### **1. Individual Resistance: The Fear of Commitment**

It’s easy to say “I should do that.” It’s harder to say “I’m doing that every day.”

Breathing well, like any practice, demands consistency. And consistency demands commitment. That’s what many people really resist—not breathing, but showing up. Day after day. Quietly. With no external reward but clarity.

We often frame our lack of practice as laziness or lack of time. But the truth might be more confronting: we resist the very notion of committing to ourselves. And that’s something worth pausing to reflect on.

In a way, your breath becomes a mirror: how you treat it reflects how you treat yourself. If you neglect it, ignore it, override it—what else in your life are you avoiding responsibility for?

### **2. Structural Resistance: The Economics of the Mindless**

Zoom out. Beyond personal habits, look at the structure we operate in.

Business, as we know it, evolved under economic theories like neoclassical economics: a world of rational agents, interchangeable consumers, and measurable outputs. These models didn’t account for presence, emotion, or breath. They couldn’t. They were designed for scale, not for self.

Even as behavioral economics has gained traction, and as mindfulness creeps into MBA electives, the core operating system still treats the individual as a variable to be optimized, not a soul to be respected.

So when mindfulness or conscious breathing enters the corporate space, it’s often labeled a “soft skill.” A nice-to-have. Something you put on slide 42 of a leadership offsite, after the revenue forecast.

This marginalization doesn’t reflect the power of the practice. It reflects the rigidity of the system.

### **3. Cultural Resistance: The East-West Dislocation**

There’s another layer, less obvious but just as potent.

Breathing, as a practice, carries a lineage. In the West, we don’t have a widespread tradition of breathing as a pathway to clarity. That lineage lives in Eastern philosophies—yogic pranayama, Zen meditation, Taoist energy work. And that creates a psychological gap.

Many in the West perceive these practices as “other.” Foreign. Exotic. Even spiritual in a way that feels disconnected from their professional identity.

In business, identity matters. We want to feel grounded in our culture, not alienated from it. So even when the practice resonates, there’s often hesitation: Will this make me seem flaky? Will I still be taken seriously? Is this me?

The irony is that these practices aim precisely to dissolve rigid identity and bring us back to our human center. But to get there, one must first be willing to risk appearing “different.”

**What breathing makes possible**

Breathing invites something rare: alignment.

Not just personal alignment (body, mind, emotion), but group alignment too. When one person breathes well, listens, pauses, and responds—they create coherence. They become a tuning fork for the room.

This is what I find most powerful: the resonance. A mindful breath isn’t just good for you—it calibrates the space. It’s one of the few acts where what benefits the individual benefits the group in equal measure. That’s rare. That’s precious.

In a world where so many incentives are misaligned, here is one that isn’t. Breathing well is good business.

**What I’m doing now**

I’ve stopped trying to “practice breathing.”

All I’m doing now is breathing—and letting the rhythm of my breath guide what I do.

When I start something, I begin with a conscious breath.

Not a ritual, but a moment of clarity—a breath that asks and opens to what I want to achieve.

When I feel resistance, I breathe into it.

Before I reach for solutions, strategies, or distractions, I use breath as my first tool to meet what’s in front of me. It doesn’t always dissolve the problem, but it always resets my stance.

**Final thought**

Breathing isn’t a technique. It’s a stance.

It’s how you meet the moment.

It’s how you show up—for yourself, and for those around you.

It’s free. It’s accessible. It’s natural. And yet it requires everything from you.

So take a breath.

Not because you need it.

But because it’s the first thing you’ve avoided all day.
