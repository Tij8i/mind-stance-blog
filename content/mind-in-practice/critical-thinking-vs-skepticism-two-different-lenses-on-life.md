*In this post, I explore a subtle but powerful distinction: critical thinking versus skepticism. Both are often used interchangeably, yet they lead us to very different places in life and in our decisions.*

A healthy dose of critical thinking is advisable in life. This distinction draws our attention to the “healthy” bit in the sentence you just read.

Is there such a thing as too much critical thinking? And what is the connection between critical thinking and skepticism?

In my experience, I would argue that, indeed, too much critical thinking will not serve us well. I do not mean to imply that we should brush over concepts and apply only superficial attention and reasoning to topics. In fact, one might argue that critical thinking can only be exercised in a fundamental way either you have done your due diligence or you have not; there is no in-between.

I do not argue with that, but at the same time, I want to remark that it is one thing to gather data and create logical structures extensively, whilst it is something different altogether to then force these structures as the only tool we have to make a decision.

A healthy dose of critical thinking requires both a strong, rigorous logic and an appreciation of the very limits of a life lived purely rationally, out of our minds.

To lead a balanced, fulfilling life, we want to be full human beings, with an integrated mind-heart-stomach approach – or however you want to slice up human experience. Our perception, our judgments, and our decisions must take all dimensions into account if we are not to become an imperfect version of a logic-seeking calculator.

As humans, we are meaning-making machines. This is not just poetry. In psychology, George Kelly’s Personal Construct Theory proposes that our reality is constructed through the meanings we assign to events. Viktor Frankl, who survived the Holocaust, built his entire practice of logotherapy around this: meaning is not rational alone. Meaning is how we continue to choose life in the face of its absurdity.

The question lies herein: is meaning something that interests only logic? What can we define as meaning in the context of human experience? Meaning is ultimately a subjective concept, so we all get to answer for ourselves. Yet in my book, on my path, meaning goes well beyond what we can rationally understand.

This is the point of what I mean by a healthy dose of critical thinking.

Now to the topic of skepticism. Skepticism has a different quality altogether. It is about how we approach life, and what we expect from unknown things. Being skeptical implies starting with the assumption that a certain object will be faulty, negative, or false unless proven otherwise. Skepticism is rooted in a lack of trust in what we are presented with.

There are good reasons to be skeptical. Paul Kurtz, founder of the Committee for Skeptical Inquiry, argued that skepticism is a method to seek truth, yet he warned of it turning into cynicism, a habitual rejection of anything new. Sometimes this mind stance can serve us well. We cannot possibly have an opinion on all topics, and when presented with new information, we apply skepticism as a heuristic to jump-start our judgment, to arrive at a conclusion faster.

We associate the new reality with something we know already and that we have decided is fishy, and based on that association, we take the perspective that even this new topic will be fishy unless proven otherwise.

There is a big problem with this approach, however. It relies on the analogy between something we have judged in the past and something new that we do not know. This means we could be basing our judgment on a wrong judgment we made in the past, or on a wrong initial impression we have about this new topic we know little about, which called upon the association in the first place.

Or, thirdly, we could even simply be using the association because our mind is under stress, overwhelmed, or lazy, and is looking for a quick way out to move on.

Daniel Kahneman’s work on System 1 and System 2 thinking (*Thinking, Fast and Slow*) illustrates this elegantly: our fast brain wants shortcuts. Skepticism is a shortcut. Critical thinking is the full path.

Furthermore, our mind often tends to disguise skepticism as critical thinking, providing a rational veil to our irrational or flawed conclusions. This helps us accept the conclusion more easily and lock in the judgment. No problem, solved!

Imagine standing on a cliff looking at the horizon. Critical thinking builds a sturdy railing to protect you while letting you admire the view. Skepticism builds a thick concrete wall, blocking the scenery just in case you fall.

These are, in my opinion, very good reasons to set an alarm bell in our mind and hear it ring out loud whenever we realize that we are being skeptical about something. As said above, I am not advocating for relinquishing your past experiences and mental heuristics altogether, but it would serve us all well if we were to be more aware of what part of our brain is making the decision and leading us in a certain direction, and how so.

When we notice that bell, we get to choose: is this skepticism or critical thinking? Is this closing down or opening up to a deeper truth? Am I reasoning or just defending my fear?

This, to me, is the practice of living with an integrated mind–logic sharpened by truth, softened by curiosity, and guided by something deeper than any argument can reach.

## **Conclusion**

Critical thinking is our tool to build understanding. Skepticism is a stance that protects us, sometimes too well. To live fully, we need both – but we must know which one we are using, and when it is time to put it down.
