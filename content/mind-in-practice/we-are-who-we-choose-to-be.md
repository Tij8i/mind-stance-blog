*In this post, I explore the idea that personality is not fixed but chosen, often unconsciously, and how awareness allows us to reshape who we are with greater freedom and intention.*

We are who we are. Or at least that’s the version of ourselves we’ve learned to defend.

What I’ve always loved about education, especially leadership and “success” education, is that it stretches the mind. It pushes me to see differently, to step outside my default interpretations. When I’m exposed to new models of success or to alternative ways of leading, there is always this subtle reminder that how I show up is not a destiny. It’s a choice. Sometimes a conscious one, often not.

This is something we rarely acknowledge: we became who we are through thousands of small, adaptive decisions. Most were made long before we had language to describe them. These decisions solidified into personality traits. And then we forgot they were decisions at all.

We talk about character as if it were a hardwired fact. But that’s not how humans work.

As we grow, we adjust ourselves to our environment. We develop strategies and ways of coping, belonging, staying safe. With time, those strategies start to look like “me.” We call them personality. But the truth is more subtle: our personality is a map of our past adaptations. It is not our essence.

Some of the biggest breakthroughs in life happen when we finally see the adaptive logic behind our behaviour. When we realise that many of the patterns we’ve been loyal to were created by a younger version of us, responding to a specific context. And that if we had grown up in different circumstances, we could easily have become someone else entirely. That’s both unsettling and incredibly freeing.

The more I pay attention to this, the more it seems obvious: if we want to evolve as human beings, part of our work is to step beyond our conditioning. Not to discard it, but to stop being defined by it. This is part of why clinging to the ego, the fixed identity, is such a trap. The ego is simply the accumulated content of our past strategies. But our deeper self, the one that perceives, the one that chooses, this is not defined by any trait. This deeper presence is stable. It is also spacious. It gives us room to move.

If we want to live from that deeper place, we need to loosen our grip on the personality we built on top of it.

The point I’m trying to make is simple but not easy:

**We could be very different people from the ones we became.**

And if we become aware of that fact, we can choose again.

We tend to assume that certain traits are “just who I am.” But if I look at myself honestly, even things I consider fundamental might simply be old adaptations. For example, I’ve always thought of myself as a gentle soul, someone who likes harmony, someone who avoids unnecessary conflict. For years, I took that as an expression of my essence. A spiritual quality even.

But what if that gentleness was a coping strategy I developed early on? What if kindergarten or primary school was socially chaotic, and my safest move was to be agreeable, to blend in, to stay fluid? That’s just one possibility. Maybe I could have become a bully, or been bullied. The fact is, I found a strategy that worked in that small world. And because it worked, I kept it. Later, it became my identity.

This is what we all do. We build identities around successful strategies. And then life reinforces them. The more these patterns “work,” the more we cling to them as truth.

But as adults, the environments change. The contexts change. The needs change. Yet many of the strategies stay frozen. And we forget they were ever choices.

Buddhist psychology has a very clean way of explaining this. According to Buddhist thought, there is no fixed “self” behind our patterns. There is only a process, an ongoing stream of conditioned reactions, perceptions, sensations, impulses. The term *anatta*, or non-self, doesn’t say we don’t exist. It says our existence is fluid, dynamic, and constantly shaped by conditions. And when we cling to any fixed identity, it generates tension. Because life moves, but we hold tight to a shape that no longer fits.

Under this lens, personality isn’t the essence. It’s residue. What’s essential is awareness—the part of us that sees, that notices, that chooses. That’s the part that’s alive and capable of change.

This connects strongly with what NLP teaches:

**Behaviour is not identity.**

Behaviour is something we do, not something we are.

And all behaviour—no matter how strange or dysfunctional- came from a place of usefulness at some moment in time. Every pattern is an adaptation.

One of the most powerful ideas in NLP is state flexibility. The more states we can access—calm, assertive, playful, focused—the more behavioural options we have. And the more options we have, the freer we become. In other words, freedom begins when we stop being loyal to a single state or personality. When we allow ourselves to be many things.

Leadership theory echoes this beautifully. Think about situational leadership: good leaders adapt their style to the context. They know that there isn’t one “authentic” way of leading. There is what the moment requires. Sometimes leading means being directive. Sometimes it means stepping back. Sometimes it means listening, sometimes deciding. No serious leadership coach believes that flexibility threatens authenticity. If anything, it makes a leader more whole.

So why don’t we allow ourselves that same flexibility in the rest of our lives?

Why do we cling so tightly to personal traits, punctuality, precision, and fairness, as if they defined our worth? Many of these traits aren’t moral truths. They’re long-acquired preferences. Punctuality, for example, is a social norm, not a virtue. Being meticulous is admired in certain environments but not others. “Being fair” often comes from personal values shaped by witnessing unfairness. These traits aren’t universal truths—they’re chosen.

And yet we anchor our identity in them. Because they give us stability. Predictability. A sense of being “right.” But stability comes at the cost of freedom. When we cling to these traits, we lock ourselves into narrow identities we can no longer grow out of. They become cages disguised as virtues.

The paradox is that we celebrate flexibility in leadership but resist it in our own inner life. We think adopting a new leadership style makes us more resourceful. But adopting a new personal habit, a new way of being, feels like a threat. We say things like, “That’s just not me.” But what do we mean by “me”? The strategy we learned at age five?

This is where Buddhist psychology, NLP, and leadership meet.

- Buddhist psychology invites us to observe the impermanence of identity.

- NLP invites us to examine the usefulness of our patterns and to choose new ones.

- Leadership theory invites us to expand our repertoire and act from presence, not habit.

All three point to the same insight:

**Freedom begins when we stop confusing our conditioning for our self.**

The moment we realise that our personality is a story, a strategy, a configuration—not a destiny—we regain agency. We can become more assertive without betraying who we “are.” We can become calmer without losing our edge. We can be more punctual, or less. More direct, or more fluid. We can shift, adapt, and experiment.

The deeper self—the part that perceives—is untouched by these changes.

If anything, it becomes clearer.

More spacious.

More real.

Retraining our perception to see how conditioning works is the first step toward a life lived with intention. A life guided from presence rather than habit. A life where we are not loyal to old patterns but responsive to what the moment asks of us.

We are not who we are.

We are who we choose to be—once we become conscious enough to choose.
