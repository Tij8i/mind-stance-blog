*In this post, I reflect on how I’ve learned to evaluate life opportunities less by outcomes and more by the kind of person they help me become.*

I often speak to people who are in the middle of change. They’re considering a new career, a different role, sometimes a relocation, sometimes a deeper identity shift that they can feel but not yet clearly articulate. I recognise that space very well. I’ve been there many times myself.

This post comes from watching how people approach these decisions, how I used to approach them, and how my perspective has changed over time. Not because I’ve figured it out, but because some patterns have become clearer to me through experience.

When we evaluate an opportunity, we tend to start with very practical questions. How much will I earn? What will my day-to-day look like? What kind of lifestyle does this allow? These are reasonable questions. Necessary, even. But I’ve come to believe they are not the most important ones.

The question that matters most, and that we rarely ask with enough honesty, is this: *does this opportunity bring me one step closer to where I want to go as a person?* And just as importantly: *how exactly will it do that?*

Not in abstract terms. Very concretely. What skills will I have to develop? What kind of pressure will I need to learn to tolerate? What weaknesses will be exposed? What mindset will I be forced to adopt if I want to succeed?

The corollary to this is something that took me a long time to accept: moving in the right direction is more important than finding the best possible opportunity. Action trumps reflection. Momentum matters more than optimisation.

Looking back, many of my decisions make sense only if I read them through that lens.

At the start of my career, I chose to stick with my family business. I could have gone into consulting or finance. I had good grades, decent options on the table, paths that were considered more prestigious and safer. Instead, I joined the family business and eventually moved to China.

From a purely strategic career perspective, that choice is debatable. The business eventually shut down. When I came back, I found myself less employable than peers who had followed more linear, recognisable paths. I had to rethink my options more than once.

And yet, I don’t regret that choice. Not because it “worked out” in the conventional sense—it didn’t—but because of the person it forced me to become. Living abroad, navigating ambiguity, being far from familiar reference points, learning to operate without a safety net. Those experiences shaped how I think, how I relate to uncertainty, how I assess risk. The hardship wasn’t incidental; it was formative.

Later, after my MBA, I went through a different phase. I was chasing what I thought I was supposed to chase: lucrative roles, impressive titles, positions that looked good on paper. And then I accepted a low-paid job as a business development manager in a startup.

From the outside, it looked like a step down. From the inside, it was aligned. I had done my MBA to get into tech and startups. That role was a training ground. It required adaptability, initiative, comfort with not knowing. Very quickly, it evolved into something bigger and set the direction for the following ten years of my career.

Not all bets paid off. Some things didn’t work. Some paths closed. But again, the value wasn’t just in the outcome. It was in the challenges I had to face and the internal adjustments I had to make along the way.

This is where I think many of us get stuck when making life decisions. We over-index on external metrics and under-index on internal development. We evaluate opportunities based on what they promise to give us, not on what they demand from us.

Every profession, every path, every long-term commitment comes with a mindset that must be learned if you want to thrive in it. Medicine requires responsibility and precision. Entrepreneurship requires tolerance for ambiguity and repeated failure. Artistic paths demand exposure, vulnerability, and a particular relationship with uncertainty. These are not surface attributes. They are inner postures.

In that sense, life is an inner game more than an outer one. The role is just the arena. What really determines whether we grow or stagnate is how willing we are to adapt our thinking instead of clinging to an identity that no longer fits.

This is something Timothy Gallwey articulated decades ago with the idea of the Inner Game: performance is limited far more by what happens inside us than by external constraints. Success, in that sense, is not about mastering the environment first, but about learning to manage oneself within it. A modern, action-oriented version of the ancient “know thyself.”

If you stay in the game long enough, something shifts. You stop asking whether a choice is right forever. You start asking whether it is the right *next* step. You realise that most paths can lead somewhere meaningful if you engage with them consciously. And that what matters most is staying nimble internally.

This reframes the decision entirely. If all paths can teach us something, where do we start? What is best for me now?

One anchor I’ve found useful is values. Not as slogans, but as lived realities. Different professions, and even different cultures within the same profession, embody different value systems. Some reward competition, others service. Some value hierarchy, others autonomy. Some demand visibility, others quiet mastery.

So instead of asking whether a role is prestigious or admired, we can ask whether the lifestyle, the expectations, and the way of being required by that role align with who we are trying to become. Not who we think we should be, but who we are actually willing to train ourselves to be.

This doesn’t make decisions easier. But it makes them more honest. And it shifts the focus from getting it right to moving forward deliberately.

Because ultimately, the biggest mistake I see, both in myself in the past and in people I speak with now—is waiting too long for certainty. For the perfect option. For the opportunity that removes doubt.

In my experience, that opportunity rarely comes. What comes instead is a direction. And the willingness to take a step, set expectations for ourselves, and let the path shape us as we go.
