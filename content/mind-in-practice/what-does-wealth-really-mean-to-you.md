*In this post I reflect on what wealth really means—not as a number, not as a headline, but as a personal, subjective experience. And why redefining it for yourself might be one of the most important mindset shifts you can make.*

## **You’re probably richer than you think—but not in the way you’ve been taught to measure it.**

A few days ago, I came across yet another headline: something about how Zuckerberg made an insane amount of money in a single day. Before that, it was Elon Musk and his trillionaire potential. And then, in the usual LinkedIn feed rotation, the celebration of some founder cashing out with a billion-euro exit.

It’s always the same tone. Awe. Admiration. Shock. A subtle suggestion that this is what success looks like, that these are the milestones that matter.

And I get it. These numbers are so high they become abstract. Fictional, almost. But they’re also *everywhere*. We see them, and whether we want to or not, we start shaping our own goals, our own definitions of success, around them. As if those stories were the natural endpoint of anyone ambitious or smart enough.

That’s where the distortion starts.

## **Money is not the problem—but it’s not the full story either.**

Let me be clear: I like money. I like what it enables. I enjoy the peace of mind that comes from not worrying about rent, or being able to say yes to a trip or a book or a tool without overthinking it. Money is a real tool.

But that’s just it—it’s a *tool*. And somewhere along the way, many of us started mistaking the tool for the goal. We conflate wealth with money. But more than that, we’ve narrowed wealth down to *extreme* money. The kind of money you can’t even reasonably spend.

And in doing that, we’ve lost track of a far more important question:

> What is wealth, really, for me?

Not the abstract, social definition.

Not the version that earns claps on LinkedIn.

But the version that makes *your* life feel full, meaningful, prosperous.

## **The problem with borrowed goals**

There’s a simple story that’s helped me put this into perspective. It’s about a man who finds a genie. The genie offers him a new life: the big house, the beautiful partner, smart kids, the Ferrari, the yacht, the works.

The man is beaming.

Then the genie says: “And now, here’s the set of problems that come with this new life.”

And that part gets forgotten. But it’s essential.

Every life, every path, every form of wealth comes with its own set of problems. And the question is not whether you want the *stuff*—but whether you’re ready to live with the *problems* that come with it.

That’s why it’s important to ask: What does wealth mean for you *not just when everything works*—but when things are hard? When your day is full of small choices and trade-offs?

Is it time? Flexibility? Energy? Peace of mind? Creativity? Relationships?

Because those are also forms of wealth. And sometimes they don’t go well with the kind of life that generates financial billions.

## **Some kinds of wealth are not counted—but deeply felt**

I’m writing this post not because I have an answer, but because I’m doing the same reflection myself. If I don’t question it, I find myself falling back into the assumption that wealth is something I *don’t* have yet. Something that’s always somewhere else. On someone else’s profile. In someone else’s press release.

But when I slow down, I notice something different. There are areas of my life that feel rich right now. Maybe not by others’ standards, but by mine.

I have mornings where I can go for a walk in silence.

I have people I love and feel safe with.

I get to choose what kind of work I want to do next.

I have the energy and freedom to try, fail, learn, and grow.

That’s wealth. Maybe not the kind with a yacht parked outside. But it’s the kind that makes me feel human, alive, and on track.

## **So how do we shift the mindset?**

Here’s a few thoughts that help me when I find myself getting swept up in the world’s definition of wealth:

- Wealth is subjective. If you don’t define it, someone else will.

- Not all wealth is monetary. Some of the richest people I’ve met live very simply.

- Money is a means, not the meaning. It’s useful, but not sufficient.

- Comparison is a trap. Especially when you compare your internal life to someone else’s external signals.

- Define your “enough”. Knowing your line helps you stay sane in a world that never stops pushing for more.

## **Wrapping up**

Wealth is not a number.

It’s a state.

It’s a stance.

It’s a story you tell yourself about what you value—and what kind of life feels worth living.

If you don’t choose that story consciously, you’ll end up living someone else’s.

So take the time to ask:

What’s my version of prosperity?

What do I already have?

And what do I want more of—not to impress the world, but to nourish my life?

That’s a better question to build from than just “How do I get rich?”
