*In this post I reflect on the tension between projecting confidence and staying rooted in authenticity.*

In the startup world you hear the phrase “fake it till you make it” all the time. The idea is that you should hold a vision so strong, so clear, that you already live as the founder who has “made it.” Not from a place of doubt or scarcity, but from certainty. From possibility.

And there’s truth in that. You want to keep your eyes on the big picture, on what you’re building, not on the cracks in the pavement. You want to cultivate the mindset that no matter what obstacle shows up, you’ll find a way through. That’s what gives you momentum. That’s what draws people in.

But in practice, “fake it till you make it” often degenerates into something shallow. It becomes a mask. A denial of struggle. A parade of celebration posts that don’t leave room for the darker, more complicated reality behind the curtain. That’s when it tips over into inauthenticity.

This is where the balance becomes tricky. On one hand, you need the conviction and energy of someone who sees the future before anyone else does. On the other hand, if that energy turns into posturing, you lose touch with reality. And when you lose touch with reality, you also lose the ability to truly connect with others.

I’ve noticed this not just with famous billionaires—who often speak as though they live in another dimension—but also with entrepreneurs closer to home. My father. My uncle. Myself. There’s this subtle air of “differentness.” Not always arrogance, but an awareness: entrepreneurs live in a world of possibility, of creation, of futures not yet built. And that mindset can make you feel separated from those who are living more in the day-to-day grind.

It’s not that the life of an entrepreneur is easier—it can be brutal, even merciless—but it’s framed by a conviction that tomorrow can be different. That’s the power of vision. And yet, here’s the paradox: the same vision that fuels you can also isolate you.

Authenticity is the counterweight. But here’s where I’ve come to a different conclusion than what most people mean when they say “authentic.” Authenticity isn’t simply about being relatable. It’s not just about showing your cracks so others can nod in sympathy. Authenticity, to me, is about being grounded in your own humanity. It’s about not letting your ego push you onto a pedestal where you forget you’re just another human being among humans.

Because when success comes, ego comes with it. That’s almost inevitable. And ego is the quickest way to slip out of authenticity—not because you’re lying, but because you stop inhabiting the part of yourself that others can meet eye-to-eye.

So where does that leave me with “fake it till you make it”?

I think it’s “yes to both.” Yes, you need the forward-leaning vision, the mindset that the future is already real. Without that, you don’t have the stamina to keep going. But at the same time, that very mindset requires even more deliberate effort to stay grounded. To keep your humanity intact. To resist ego.

Otherwise, you might succeed, you might even “stay authentic” to your self-image, but you won’t be authentic to the humanity in you. And people feel that. You’ll feel it too.
